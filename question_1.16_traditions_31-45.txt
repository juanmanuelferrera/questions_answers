*** 1.16.31 METHODIST (HOLINESS) Response

Do we have free will? The Holiness tradition, rooted in John Wesley's theology and emphasized through the 19th-century holiness movement, affirms human free will as essential to both salvation and sanctification. We possess genuine moral agency that enables us to respond to God's prevenient grace and pursue entire sanctification.

Wesley taught that while humanity fell through Adam's sin, God's prevenient grace restores to all people the capacity to choose or reject salvation. This grace precedes our conscious choice, preventing absolute determinism while avoiding Pelagian self-sufficiency. Free will functions neither as autonomous power nor complete bondage, but as grace-enabled capacity. Without prevenient grace, we would remain enslaved to sin; with it, we can freely respond to God's call.

The holiness emphasis on entire sanctification particularly requires robust free will. Following regeneration, believers must choose consecration—a deliberate surrender of the entire self to God. This choice, empowered by the Holy Spirit, leads to sanctifying grace that purifies the heart from inbred sin. The testimony of holiness pioneers like Phoebe Palmer demonstrates that sanctification requires conscious, willful cooperation with divine grace. We must "lay all on the altar" through deliberate choice.

However, our freedom operates within grace's framework. The Holy Spirit convicts, draws, and empowers, but never coerces. Scriptural support appears throughout: Joshua's challenge to "choose this day whom you will serve" (Joshua 24:15) presumes genuine choice. Jesus's invitation "whoever will, let him take the water of life freely" (Revelation 22:17) assumes human agency. Peter's declaration that God "is patient toward you, not willing that any should perish but that all should come to repentance" (2 Peter 3:9) indicates divine desire limited by human response.

The practical implications profoundly shape holiness life. Believers must vigilantly guard their consecration through daily choices. We can grieve the Holy Spirit, resist sanctifying grace, or backslide through willful sin. Conversely, we can grow in grace, pursue deeper consecration, and maintain victory over sin through Spirit-empowered choices.

Critics argue this framework grants too much human capacity, but we maintain God's sovereignty establishes rather than eliminates meaningful freedom. The Spirit's enabling precedes every right choice, ensuring God receives glory for salvation while human responsibility remains real. Free will, properly understood as grace-restored agency, harmonizes divine sovereignty with human accountability, enabling the transformative holiness God desires for all believers.

*** 1.16.32 PENTECOSTAL (CLASSICAL) Response

Do we have free will? Classical Pentecostalism, emerging from the Azusa Street Revival and rooted in Wesleyan-Holiness theology, emphatically affirms free will as central to both soteriology and pneumatology. We possess genuine moral agency to accept or reject God's grace, baptism in the Holy Spirit, and the Spirit's ongoing direction in our lives.

The Pentecostal understanding begins with humanity's creation in God's image, which necessarily includes volitional capacity. The Fall damaged but did not destroy this capacity. Through prevenient grace, God restores to all people sufficient ability to respond to the gospel invitation. This restoration makes evangelism meaningful—we preach because people can genuinely choose Christ, and their choices have eternal consequences.

The distinctive Pentecostal emphasis on Spirit baptism particularly requires robust free will. Following conversion, believers must hunger for, seek, and receive the Holy Spirit's fullness. The early Pentecostals spent hours in tarrying meetings, seeking this experience through persistent prayer and yielded will. Testimonies consistently describe a moment of full surrender—"letting go and letting God"—that preceded Spirit baptism. This surrender represents genuine human choice, though empowered by divine grace.

Moreover, living the Spirit-filled life demands continuous cooperation with the Holy Spirit. The gifts of the Spirit (1 Corinthians 12) operate through willing human vessels. Prophecy requires yielding one's voice; interpretation demands submission of understanding; healing flows through obedient prayer. Paul's instruction to "quench not the Spirit" (1 Thessalonians 5:19) assumes we can choose to resist or cooperate with divine promptings.

Biblical foundation permeates Pentecostal teaching. Peter's Pentecost sermon climaxes with "whoever calls on the name of the Lord shall be saved" (Acts 2:21), presuming human choice. Philip's question to the Ethiopian eunuch, "Do you believe?" (Acts 8:37), requires volitional response. Paul's exhortation to "be filled with the Spirit" (Ephesians 5:18) uses the imperative mood, commanding willing obedience.

However, Pentecostals maintain that authentic freedom derives from grace, not nature. The Spirit convicts sinners, draws seekers, and empowers saints, but never forces submission. Every genuine work of God requires human cooperation. We can resist the Spirit, grieve the Spirit, or quench the Spirit through willful choices.

This framework shapes Pentecostal practice profoundly. Altar calls invite decision; prayer rooms facilitate seeking; testimonies emphasize personal choice. The urgency of evangelism intensifies precisely because people's eternal destinies hinge on their free responses to God's gracious invitation. Free will, restored by grace and empowered by the Spirit, enables the dynamic, experiential faith that characterizes Pentecostal Christianity.

*** 1.16.33 PENTECOSTAL (ONENESS) Response

Do we have free will? Oneness Pentecostalism, distinguished by its non-Trinitarian theology and emphasis on Acts 2:38 salvation, affirms genuine human free will within God's sovereign plan. We possess real moral agency to respond to the gospel, receive the Spirit, and maintain our salvation through ongoing obedient choices.

The Oneness perspective begins with God's absolute singularity—Jesus Christ is the one God manifested in flesh. This singular God, in His sovereignty, created humanity with genuine volitional capacity. Though sin enslaved humanity, God's redemptive grace, manifested in Jesus Christ, restores the ability to choose salvation. The preaching of the apostolic gospel (death, burial, and resurrection of Jesus) confronts people with a decision requiring genuine free will.

The distinctive Oneness soteriology particularly emphasizes human response. Salvation requires three observable components: repentance (a willful turning from sin), water baptism in Jesus' name (an obedient choice), and receiving the Holy Spirit with the evidence of speaking in tongues (a yielded reception). Each component demands human cooperation. Peter's command "Repent, and be baptized every one of you in the name of Jesus Christ" (Acts 2:38) addresses individual will. Repentance cannot be coerced—it must be freely chosen. Similarly, baptism requires willing submission to apostolic authority and biblical pattern.

Receiving the Holy Spirit, while entirely God's gift, also requires human cooperation. Seekers must hunger, pray, yield, and receive. The initial evidence of tongues manifests as the Spirit gives utterance, but the believer must choose to speak. This synergy between divine empowerment and human response characterizes authentic Pentecostal experience. We can resist, we can grieve, or we can quench the Spirit through our choices.

Furthermore, Oneness theology emphasizes conditional security—believers can forfeit salvation through willful sin or apostasy. This doctrine necessarily requires robust free will. The warnings throughout Scripture against falling away presume believers possess genuine capacity to choose rebellion. Hebrews' warning about trampling the Son of God underfoot (Hebrews 10:29) assumes volitional apostasy. Paul's concern that he might become a castaway (1 Corinthians 9:27) reveals authentic possibility of spiritual failure through wrong choices.

However, our freedom operates entirely within grace's provision. God's sovereignty establishes human freedom rather than eliminating it. Every right choice occurs through grace's enablement; every rejection of grace demonstrates humanity's tragic capacity for self-destruction.

The practical implications shape Oneness practice significantly. Evangelistic urgency intensifies because people's choices determine eternal destiny. Holiness standards call believers to daily choices reflecting transformation. Prayer meetings emphasize seeking God with deliberate persistence. Free will, properly understood as grace-enabled agency exercised within God's sovereign plan, enables the obedient, Spirit-filled life commanded in apostolic doctrine.

*** 1.16.34 PENTECOSTAL (NEO-CHARISMATIC) Response

Do we have free will? Neo-charismatic Christianity, characterized by contextual flexibility and emphasis on spiritual gifts outside traditional Pentecostal structures, affirms human free will as essential to faith, spiritual growth, and Kingdom advancement. We possess genuine agency to partner with God's purposes while remaining entirely dependent on His grace.

The neo-charismatic perspective embraces a dynamic understanding of divine-human cooperation. God's sovereignty encompasses rather than excludes human freedom. This paradox, while mysterious, reflects biblical testimony and spiritual experience. God reigns absolutely, yet genuinely invites human participation in His redemptive work. Prayer changes outcomes, faith releases miracles, and obedience advances the Kingdom—all presupposing meaningful human agency.

Contemporary charismatic theology particularly emphasizes believers' authority in Christ. Through union with Jesus, we receive delegated authority over demonic powers, sickness, and circumstances. This authority remains meaningless without free will to exercise it. Declarations of faith, prophetic decrees, and spiritual warfare all require volitional human engagement. We must choose to believe God's promises, speak forth His word, and resist the enemy. James's instruction to "resist the devil, and he will flee from you" (James 4:7) assumes genuine human agency.

The gifts of the Holy Spirit, central to neo-charismatic practice, operate through willing human cooperation. Unlike mechanical instruments, Spirit-filled believers consciously participate in prophetic revelation, words of knowledge, and healing ministry. The Spirit distributes gifts "as He wills" (1 Corinthians 12:11), yet operates through yielded human vessels who must choose cooperation. We can quench promptings, resist revelation, or embrace divine flow through our choices.

Moreover, neo-charismatic emphasis on transformation and Kingdom living requires robust free will. Believers must choose to renew their minds (Romans 12:2), take thoughts captive (2 Corinthians 10:5), and present bodies as living sacrifices. These imperatives presume volitional capacity. Spiritual formation occurs through Spirit-empowered human choices—disciplines pursued, habits formed, and character developed through deliberate cooperation with grace.

Biblical warrant saturates this framework. Jesus's invitation "follow me" requires volitional response. The Great Commission to "make disciples" assumes people can choose belief. Paul's exhortation to "work out your salvation with fear and trembling, for it is God who works in you" (Philippians 2:12-13) perfectly balances divine sovereignty and human responsibility.

However, neo-charismatics recognize that authentic freedom derives from grace. Apart from the Spirit's drawing, conviction, and empowerment, we remain enslaved to sin. Every right choice reflects grace at work; every Kingdom advance glorifies God alone.

This understanding shapes neo-charismatic practice profoundly. Believers are equipped as ministers, not mere recipients. Small groups emphasize participatory ministry. Worship cultivates responsive hearts. Testimonies celebrate God's power working through human obedience. Free will, understood as grace-enabled partnership with God's purposes, facilitates the dynamic, transformative faith that characterizes contemporary charismatic Christianity.

*** 1.16.35 ANABAPTIST (MENNONITE) Response

Do we have free will? Mennonite theology, rooted in the radical reformation and Anabaptist convictions, affirms free will as essential to genuine discipleship, voluntary church membership, and the costly obedience Jesus demands. We possess real moral agency to hear Christ's call, choose baptism, and follow the narrow way of kingdom living.

The Mennonite understanding begins with Jesus's central teaching: the Kingdom of God has broken into history, demanding radical response. This Kingdom comes not through coercion but invitation. Christ calls disciples who must freely choose to follow—counting the cost, forsaking lesser loyalties, and embracing cross-bearing obedience. The Sermon on the Mount addresses human will directly, commanding choices that distinguish Kingdom citizens from surrounding culture: love enemies, refuse violence, practice radical generosity, pursue purity. These imperatives presume genuine volitional capacity.

Historically, early Anabaptists rejected infant baptism precisely because it violated free will. Baptism must follow conscious decision—repentance from sin and commitment to follow Jesus in community. The Schleitheim Confession (1527) articulated believers' baptism as voluntary covenant with Christ and His body. Coerced faith contradicts authentic Christianity. The thousands of Anabaptist martyrs testified to this conviction, choosing death over compulsion. Their sacrifices make sense only if free will matters eternally.

Mennonite ecclesiology similarly requires robust free agency. The church consists of voluntary members who covenant together for mutual accountability and discipleship. Church discipline, practiced according to Matthew 18, assumes members can choose conformity or rebellion. The ban (excommunication) serves to preserve community purity while respecting individual agency—members who persistently choose sin separate themselves from fellowship.

Furthermore, Mennonite peace theology necessarily depends on free will. Nonviolence and enemy love require deliberate choice against natural inclination and cultural pressure. The choice to refuse military service, absorb injustice without retaliation, and pursue reconciliation demonstrates human capacity for Spirit-empowered counter-cultural obedience. Jesus's command to "love your enemies" (Matthew 5:44) addresses will, not feeling—a choice to seek others' good despite hostility.

Biblical support permeates Mennonite thought. Jesus's invitation "If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross and follow me" (Mark 8:34) requires volitional response. The parable of the two builders concludes with individual choice to obey or ignore Jesus's words (Matthew 7:24-27). Paul's exhortation "Do not be conformed to this world" (Romans 12:2) commands willful resistance to cultural formation.

However, Mennonites recognize that faithful choices occur through grace. The Spirit enables obedience impossible for fallen humanity alone. Community provides support, accountability, and encouragement for choices that conflict with worldly wisdom. Free will, properly understood as grace-restored capacity exercised within faithful community, enables authentic discipleship and Kingdom witness.

*** 1.16.36 ANABAPTIST (AMISH) Response

Do we have free will? The Amish tradition, emphasizing Gelassenheit (yieldedness) and communal discernment, affirms human free will while subordinating individual autonomy to collective wisdom and divine sovereignty. We possess genuine moral agency, exercised rightly through submission to God, church, and Ordnung (communal order).

The Amish understanding recognizes humanity's creation with volitional capacity. God made us capable of choice, evidenced in Adam and Eve's tragic decision to disobey. This capacity persists after the Fall, though corrupted by sin. Every person faces fundamental choices: to accept or reject Christ, to join or refuse the church, to submit to or rebel against community standards. These choices carry eternal and temporal consequences.

However, the Amish carefully distinguish between worldly autonomy and baptized will. Modern individualism, which exalts personal preference above communal wisdom and divine command, represents sinful pride. True freedom emerges through Gelassenheit—voluntary self-surrender to God's will as discerned through Scripture, tradition, and community. The highest exercise of free will is willing submission to Christ's lordship and the church's collective discernment.

Rumspringa, the period when Amish youth experience greater freedom before choosing baptism, demonstrates this theology practically. Young people genuinely choose whether to join the church or leave for worldly life. The community respects this choice, though it hopes for faithful commitment. Those who choose baptism covenant to obey the Ordnung and accept church discipline. This covenant represents free will's most significant exercise—voluntary submission to communal Christian life.

The practice of church discipline similarly requires free will. When members violate Ordnung, the church calls them to repentance and, if necessary, institutes the ban (Meidung). This discipline respects human agency while protecting community purity. Members choose conformity or consequences. The goal remains restoration—helping wayward members freely choose renewed obedience.

Biblical warrant includes Jesus's teaching that "whoever would save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life for my sake will find it" (Matthew 16:25). True freedom comes through self-surrender, not self-assertion. Paul's instruction to "submit to one another out of reverence for Christ" (Ephesians 5:21) establishes mutual submission as Christian norm. The Jerusalem Council's discernment process (Acts 15) models communal decision-making that values collective wisdom.

The Amish recognize that grace enables right choices. Apart from God's drawing, people remain enslaved to pride and worldliness. The Spirit works through community, Scripture, and conscience to guide choices toward faithful obedience.

Practically, this framework shapes Amish life profoundly. Technology choices, clothing standards, and occupational decisions all reflect communal discernment rather than individual preference. Children receive training in submission before experiencing decision-making responsibility. Free will, understood as grace-enabled capacity rightly exercised through Gelassenheit and communal accountability, preserves both personal agency and faithful community in an individualistic age.

*** 1.16.37 ANABAPTIST (BRETHREN) Response

Do we have free will? The Brethren tradition, emphasizing simple obedience to New Testament teaching and the way of peace, affirms genuine human free will as essential to voluntary discipleship and covenant community. We possess real moral agency to hear Christ's call, choose baptism by trine immersion, and follow the path of humble service.

The Brethren understanding begins with Jesus's invitation to follow Him in radical obedience. The Kingdom of God advances not through force but persuasion, not through coercion but conversion. Christ calls disciples who must freely choose costly obedience—rejecting violence, pursuing simplicity, practicing mutual aid, and embracing servant leadership. These commitments require genuine volitional capacity, not forced compliance.

Alexander Mack and early Brethren rejected both Calvinist predestination and infant baptism because both violated free will. They insisted that saving faith requires conscious decision. Baptism, administered to believing adults by forward trine immersion, signifies voluntary covenant with Christ and community. The candidate freely chooses to die to sin, be buried with Christ, and rise to new life. This ordinance loses meaning if human choice lacks reality.

Brethren ecclesiology similarly depends on robust free agency. The church consists of covenanted believers who voluntarily commit to mutual accountability and simple obedience. The annual Love Feast, including foot washing, fellowship meal, and communion, embodies this voluntary covenant. Members choose participation, demonstrating renewed commitment to Christ and one another. Church discipline, practiced according to Matthew 18, respects human agency while maintaining community standards. Members can choose repentance or separation.

The distinctive Brethren peace testimony necessarily requires free will. The decision to refuse military service, practice enemy love, and pursue reconciliation often conflicts with cultural expectations and personal inclination. Yet Jesus's command to "love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you" (Matthew 5:44) addresses human will directly. The Brethren peace position makes sense only if people genuinely can choose nonviolence despite societal pressure toward violence.

Moreover, Brethren emphasis on simple living and mutual aid requires continuous volitional commitment. Believers must choose generosity over accumulation, service over status, and community welfare over individual advantage. These choices, while Spirit-enabled, reflect genuine human agency cooperating with divine grace.

Biblical support includes Jesus's Sermon on the Mount, which consistently addresses human will: "Let your light shine," "Give to the one who begs," "Do not resist the one who is evil" (Matthew 5:16, 42, 39). These imperatives presume volitional capacity. Paul's instruction to "outdo one another in showing honor" (Romans 12:10) commands deliberate choice against natural self-preference.

However, Brethren recognize that right choices flow from transformed hearts. The Spirit enables obedience impossible for unregenerate will. Community provides encouragement, accountability, and practical support for choices that contradict worldly wisdom. Free will, properly understood as grace-restored capacity exercised within covenant community, enables the simple, peaceful, obedient life Jesus modeled and commanded.

*** 1.16.38 QUAKER (CONSERVATIVE) Response

Do we have free will? Conservative Friends, maintaining traditional Quaker emphasis on the Inward Light while preserving Christ-centered theology, affirm genuine human free will as essential to faithful listening and obedient response to the Spirit's guidance. We possess real moral agency to heed or resist the Light that enlightens everyone.

The Quaker understanding begins with John 1:9—Christ is "the true Light, which enlightens every person coming into the world." This divine Light shines within all people, illuminating conscience and drawing toward truth. However, the Light's presence does not compel obedience. Each person faces genuine choice: to follow the Light toward life, or resist it toward spiritual death. Early Friends insisted that God's grace appears to all, making salvation universally available, yet conditioned on human response.

George Fox's journals repeatedly emphasize human responsibility to obey revealed truth. His opening of Scriptures and convincement of sin occurred through the Light's inward working, yet required his willing response. The witness of early Friends to their convincement describes genuine struggle between self-will and divine will, resolved through freely chosen surrender to Truth. Persecuted Friends chose faithfulness despite imprisonment, loss of property, and even death—choices meaningless without free will.

The practice of waiting worship particularly requires robust free agency. Friends gather in expectant silence, attending to the Spirit's promptings. When moved to speak, one must discern whether the prompting originates from divine Source or human impulse. The decision to speak or remain silent, to obey or resist the inward motion, reflects genuine human choice. The query "Is this of the Lord or of self?" acknowledges our capacity to mistake human agenda for divine leading.

Business meetings conducted under the Spirit's guidance similarly depend on free will. Friends seek corporate discernment of God's will, not majority preference. Yet this process requires individuals to surrender personal opinions, listen deeply to others, and embrace unity even when it conflicts with initial positions. Each Friend chooses whether to persist in individual judgment or yield to emerging sense of meeting. True unity arises through voluntary submission to divine leading, not forced conformity.

Biblical warrant includes Jesus's question "Why do you call me 'Lord, Lord,' and not do what I tell you?" (Luke 6:46), which presumes capacity to hear and disobey. The parable of the sower describes varied responses to gospel proclamation, indicating genuine human receptivity or resistance. Paul's instruction to "test everything; hold fast what is good" (1 Thessalonians 5:21) requires active discernment.

However, Conservative Friends maintain that faithful choices occur through grace. The Light itself enables recognition of truth and empowers obedience. Apart from inward illumination, we remain in darkness, incapable of finding the Way. Free will, properly understood as grace-enabled response to the Inward Christ, allows faithful obedience and spiritual transformation grounded in direct experience of divine Reality.

*** 1.16.39 QUAKER (LIBERAL) Response

Liberal Friends, emphasizing continuing revelation and respect for individual spiritual journey, affirm human free will as foundational to authentic spiritual seeking and ethical responsibility. We possess genuine agency to pursue truth, respond to that of God within everyone, and act according to conscience and inner Light.

The liberal Quaker perspective recognizes free will as essential to personal spiritual integrity. Religious truth cannot be imposed externally through creed, dogma, or authority—it must be freely discovered through direct experience and inward seeking. The Light within (understood variously as conscience, divine spark, or ultimate reality) illuminates truth, but each person chooses their response. Coerced belief contradicts authentic spirituality. Faith must emerge through genuine seeking, questioning, and personal convincement.

Liberal Friends particularly emphasize individual conscience as sacred. While community provides wisdom, accountability, and support, ultimate moral authority resides within each person's direct access to the Light. When convinced of truth, individuals must follow their leading even if it conflicts with collective opinion. Historical Friends like John Woolman exemplify this principle—his witness against slavery arose from personal conscience faithfully followed despite economic and social pressure. Such prophetic witness requires robust free will to resist conformity and obey inner truth.

The practice of expectant waiting in worship depends entirely on free will. Friends gather without programmed liturgy, creating space for Spirit to move. Each person chooses their inward posture—openness or resistance, attentiveness or distraction, self-emptying or self-preoccupation. Vocal ministry arises when individuals freely choose to share inward leadings. The decision to speak or remain silent reflects genuine human agency.

Moreover, liberal Quaker social testimonies—peace, equality, integrity, simplicity, community—require continuous volitional commitment. These values are not imposed rules but freely chosen responses to spiritual conviction. The decision to refuse military service, practice radical equality, or live simply reflects individual conscience informed by collective wisdom. Friends act from conviction, not compulsion.

Liberal Friends find biblical support in varied scriptural testimonies to human responsibility. The prophets called Israel to choose justice. Jesus invited discipleship without coercion. Paul emphasized testing and discernment. However, liberal Friends also value non-Christian wisdom traditions that affirm human moral agency and spiritual seeking.

Critically, liberal Friends resist theological frameworks that diminish human responsibility. Determinism—whether theological predestination or scientific reductionism—contradicts moral agency and ethical accountability. If choices are predetermined, appeals to conscience, calls to justice, and practices of discernment become meaningless.

The practical implications shape liberal Quaker community profoundly. Children receive spiritual nurture, not indoctrination, trusting they will find their own authentic path. Clearness committees support individual discernment without dictating outcomes. Social witness emerges from personal conviction. Free will, understood as inherent human capacity for moral agency and spiritual seeking, enables the authentic, responsive, justice-seeking life that characterizes liberal Friends practice.

*** 1.16.40 ADVENTIST (SEVENTH-DAY) Response

Do we have free will? Seventh-day Adventist theology, rooted in the great controversy theme and emphasizing God's law and judgment, emphatically affirms human free will as central to the cosmic conflict between Christ and Satan. We possess genuine moral agency whose choices have eternal significance and cosmic implications.

The Adventist understanding begins with the Great Controversy—the universe-wide conflict between good and evil, Christ and Satan, truth and deception. This controversy fundamentally concerns God's character and government. Satan accused God of tyranny, claiming His law enslaves. God's response demonstrates that His government rests on love, which requires freedom. Forced obedience proves nothing; voluntary loyalty vindicates God's character. Thus free will becomes essential to the controversy's resolution.

Ellen G. White wrote extensively on human agency. While God's foreknowledge encompasses all future choices, this knowledge does not cause choices. God foresees but does not predetermine. Each person stands before divine law with genuine capacity to obey or rebel. The investigative judgment, beginning in 1844, examines how individuals exercised their freedom—whether they chose Christ's righteousness and faithful obedience, or persisted in sin.

Adventist soteriology balances divine sovereignty and human response. Salvation comes entirely through Christ's righteousness, received by faith. Yet faith itself represents human choice—responding to the Spirit's drawing, accepting offered grace, and surrendering will to God. Once justified, believers cooperate with sanctifying grace through Spirit-empowered obedience to God's law, including the seventh-day Sabbath. This obedience flows from love, not legalism, representing willing response to God's covenant.

The investigative judgment particularly requires robust free will. Beginning with the sanctuary doctrine from Daniel 8:14, Adventists teach that Christ currently examines professed believers' lives. This judgment evaluates whether individuals genuinely chose Christ and demonstrated faith through obedient living. The examination presumes that choices matter eternally—that individuals could have chosen differently.

Biblical support saturates Adventist theology. Moses's declaration "I have set before you life and death, blessing and curse. Therefore choose life" (Deuteronomy 30:19) exemplifies divine respect for human choice. Joshua's challenge "choose this day whom you will serve" (Joshua 24:15) demands volitional commitment. Revelation's final invitation, "whoever desires, let him take the water of life freely" (Revelation 22:17), offers salvation to willing recipients.

However, Adventists recognize sin's enslaving power. Apart from Christ, we remain captive to Satan's deceptions and unable to fulfill God's law. The Spirit convicts, enlightens, and empowers, enabling choices impossible for unregenerate will. Every right choice reflects grace at work.

Practically, this framework shapes Adventist mission urgently. The three angels' messages call people to choose worship of the Creator over beast's mark. Free will, exercised in response to truth's final proclamation, determines eternal destiny and vindicates God's character before the watching universe.

*** 1.16.41 RESTORATIONIST (CHURCHES OF CHRIST) Response

Do we have free will? We possess genuine moral agency to hear the Word, believe the gospel, and obey Christ's commands.

The Restorationist understanding emphasizes individual response to clearly revealed divine truth. God has spoken definitively in Scripture, providing pattern for salvation and church practice. This revelation addresses human will, commanding specific responses: hearing, believing, repenting, confessing, and being baptized for remission of sins. These commands presume volitional capacity. Without free will, gospel imperatives become meaningless.

Alexander Campbell and early Restoration leaders rejected Calvinist predestination as contradicting Scripture's plain teaching. They insisted that salvation is offered to all who freely respond in faith and obedience. The gospel is God's power unto salvation (Romans 1:16), but effectiveness depends on human reception. The seed (Word) possesses life-giving power, but must fall on receptive soil (heart) to produce fruit. The parable of the sower illustrates varied human responses to identical divine message—some receive, others reject, based on heart condition.

Baptism particularly demonstrates the necessity of free will. The New Testament consistently links baptism with salvation: "He who believes and is baptized will be saved" (Mark 16:16); "Repent and be baptized every one of you in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins" (Acts 2:38). Furthermore, faithful Christian living requires continuous volitional commitment. New Testament imperatives command believers to assemble regularly, observe the Lord's Supper weekly, support gospel preaching, practice church discipline, and pursue holiness. These directives assume believers possess genuine agency to obey or disobey. The warnings against apostasy throughout Hebrews presume believers can choose to fall away: "Take care, brothers, lest there be in any of you an evil, unbelieving heart, leading you to fall away from the living God" (Hebrews 3:12).

Churches of Christ emphasize individual accountability. Each person must study Scripture personally, discern truth from error, and stand before God based on their own choices. Jesus's warning that "many will say to me on that day, 'Lord, Lord,'" only to be rejected (Matthew 7:21-23), underscores individual accountability for obedient response.

However, we recognize that right choices occur through God's gracious provision. The gospel itself is divine power; the Word possesses transforming efficacy; the Spirit works through Scripture to convict and convert. *** 1.16.42 RESTORATIONIST (CHRISTIAN CHURCHES) Response

The Restoration vision of Thomas and Alexander Campbell emphasized unity based on biblical essentials while allowing liberty in matters of opinion. This framework presumes meaningful human agency—the capacity to examine Scripture, discern essential truths, and choose obedient response. The famous motto "In essentials unity, in opinions liberty, in all things love" assumes believers can distinguish essentials from opinions through rational examination and spiritual discernment, then freely choose appropriate response.

Faith represents genuine human response to divine initiative, not predetermined election of limited recipients.

Baptism by immersion, practiced upon confession of faith, particularly demonstrates free will's necessity. Following New Testament pattern, baptism symbolizes and effects spiritual transformation—death to sin, burial with Christ, resurrection to new life. This ordinance requires conscious participation. The candidate chooses repentance, professes faith, and submits to immersion, actively receiving God's gracious gift of salvation. Passive recipients cannot fulfill this pattern.

The Christian Churches' congregational polity similarly requires robust free agency. Leadership arises through congregational selection of qualified individuals. Church discipline, when necessary, respects members' freedom while maintaining community standards. Evangelistic appeals invite decision, presuming volitional capacity to accept or reject Christ.

Biblical support includes Jesus's invitation "Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden" (Matthew 11:28), which addresses willing participants. The book of Acts repeatedly demonstrates individual responses to apostolic preaching—some believed, others rejected. Paul's reasoning with Felix caused the governor to become frightened, responding "Go away for the present" (Acts 24:25)—a free choice against conviction.

However, Christian Churches recognize that saving faith occurs through grace. The Spirit convicts sinners, opens blind eyes, and draws hard hearts toward Christ. Every positive response to the gospel reflects divine enablement. Free will, properly understood as grace-restored capacity to respond to God's loving invitation, harmonizes divine sovereignty with human responsibility.

Do we have free will? Latter-day Saint theology, grounded in modern revelation and distinctive cosmology, emphatically affirms human free will—termed "agency"—as eternal, essential to human nature, and central to God's plan of salvation. We possessed agency in our pre-mortal existence, exercise it during mortality, and will retain it eternally.

The LDS understanding begins before earthly creation. In the pre-mortal Council in Heaven, God presented His plan for human progression. This plan required mortality where spirits would receive physical bodies, experience opposition between good and evil, and exercise agency to choose righteousness or wickedness. Lucifer proposed an alternative plan that would force compliance and guarantee everyone's return, eliminating agency. God rejected this plan, and Lucifer rebelled, becoming Satan. The War in Heaven fundamentally concerned agency's preservation. One-third of heaven's hosts chose Satan, demonstrating that even in pre-mortal glory, spirits possessed genuine free will.

Agency constitutes an eternal principle, co-eternal with God Himself. It cannot be created or destroyed, only respected or violated. The second Article of Faith states: "We believe that men will be punished for their own sins, and not for Adam's transgression." This declaration establishes individual moral responsibility grounded in genuine volitional capacity. Each person chooses their own path and bears consequences accordingly.

The plan of salvation requires robust agency. Mortal life provides the opposition necessary for meaningful choice—good and evil, truth and error, pleasure and pain. Without opposition, agency could not operate: "For it must needs be, that there is an opposition in all things. If not so... righteousness could not be brought to pass, neither wickedness, neither holiness nor misery, neither good nor bad" (2 Nephi 2:11). Lehi taught his son Jacob that God created man free: "They are free to choose liberty and eternal life, through the great Mediator of all men, or to choose captivity and death" (2 Nephi 2:27).

Jesus Christ's atonement enables agency to operate redemptively. Sin's consequences would otherwise destroy us, but Christ's infinite sacrifice satisfies justice, allowing mercy to claim the penitent. Yet the atonement's benefits require individual acceptance through faith, repentance, baptism, receiving the Holy Ghost, and enduring faithfully. Each step requires volitional choice. God invites, persuades, and enables, but never compels.

Furthermore, LDS theology teaches continuing progression through eternities. Even exalted beings continue choosing righteousness, growing in knowledge and power. Agency persists eternally—spirits existed before mortality with agency, exercise agency during earth life, and continue exercising it in post-mortal spirit world and resurrection.

Doctrine and Covenants reveals: "All truth is independent in that sphere in which God has placed it, to act for itself, as all intelligence also; otherwise there is no existence" (D&C 93:30). This verse establishes intelligence's inherent capacity for self-directed action.

Practically, LDS emphasis on agency shapes family life, missionary work, and social ethics. Parents teach but cannot force testimony. Missionaries persuade but respect rejection. Free will, understood as eternal divine endowment central to human identity, enables the progression toward godhood that constitutes God's ultimate purpose for His children.

*** 1.16.44 RESTORATIONIST (JEHOVAH'S WITNESSES) Response

Do we have free will? Jehovah's Witnesses, committed to biblical truth as revealed through the Watchtower organization, affirm genuine human free will within Jehovah God's sovereign purposes. We possess real moral agency to accept or reject the truth, dedicate ourselves to Jehovah, and maintain faithful obedience until the end.

The Witness understanding begins with Jehovah's perfect justice, which requires moral accountability. If humans lacked genuine free will, holding them accountable for sin would contradict God's justice. Yet Scripture consistently presents humans as responsible for their choices. Adam and Eve possessed freedom to obey or disobey God's clear command regarding the forbidden tree. Their choice to eat brought sin and death into the world—a consequence meaningful only if their action represented genuine volitional rebellion.

Jehovah created humans with free will to demonstrate that His sovereignty does not depend on controlling every action. Satan challenged God's right to rule, claiming humans serve Jehovah only from selfish motives. The subsequent history provides opportunity for humans to freely choose loyalty to Jehovah or rebellion, thus answering Satan's challenge. Those who faithfully maintain integrity to Jehovah despite opposition vindicate His sovereignty and disprove Satan's accusation. Job exemplifies such faithful choice despite terrible suffering.

Salvation requires individual response to Kingdom truth. Jehovah draws truth-seekers through His organization, but individuals must freely accept accurate knowledge, reject false religious teachings, and dedicate themselves to Jehovah through water baptism. The decision to become one of Jehovah's Witnesses represents genuine volitional commitment with eternal consequences. Jesus said the road to life is narrow and few find it (Matthew 7:14), indicating that most people freely choose destruction rather than salvation.

Furthermore, maintaining faithful obedience requires continuous free choice. Witnesses must regularly attend meetings, participate in field ministry, avoid worldly contamination, and remain separate from Satan's system. These requirements demand ongoing volitional commitment. The possibility of disfellowshipping demonstrates that even baptized Witnesses can freely choose sin over faithfulness. Those who willfully practice wrongdoing after knowing truth choose their own destruction.

Biblical support permeates Witness teaching. Joshua's declaration "Choose for yourselves today whom you will serve" (Joshua 24:15) presumes genuine human agency. Deuteronomy 30:19 states: "I have put life and death before you, the blessing and the curse; and you must choose life." Jesus's invitation "Come to me, all you who are toiling and loaded down" (Matthew 11:28) addresses willing participants. The book of Revelation promises that "anyone who wishes may take the water of life without cost" (Revelation 22:17), indicating universal opportunity conditioned on individual choice.

However, Jehovah's Witnesses recognize that right choices require accurate knowledge provided through Jehovah's organization. Without truth, people remain deceived by Satan and false religion. The Governing Body provides spiritual food enabling informed decisions. Free will, exercised in response to revealed truth and within Jehovah's sovereign purposes, determines who will survive Armageddon and inherit Paradise earth.

*** 1.16.45 EVANGELICAL (FUNDAMENTALIST) Response

Do we have free will? Fundamentalist evangelicalism, committed to biblical inerrancy and doctrinal purity, affirms human free will within specific theological boundaries—though perspectives vary between more Calvinist and Arminian fundamentalists. Generally, we recognize genuine human agency in responding to God's gracious gospel invitation, while maintaining God's absolute sovereignty and Scripture's supreme authority.

The fundamentalist understanding emphasizes several non-negotiable doctrines: biblical inerrancy, Christ's virgin birth, substitutionary atonement, bodily resurrection, and literal second coming. These fundamentals establish the objective truth framework within which human will operates. God has revealed Himself propositionally in Scripture, commanding human response. The gospel addresses human will directly: "Believe on the Lord Jesus Christ, and you will be saved" (Acts 16:31). This imperative presumes volitional capacity.

More Arminian-leaning fundamentalists emphasize that salvation is genuinely offered to all people. God "desires all people to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth" (1 Timothy 2:4). Christ's atonement provides unlimited provision—sufficient for all, efficient for those who believe. The Spirit convicts all sinners, drawing them toward repentance. Yet individuals must freely respond in faith. The gospel invitation goes to all; personal choice determines who accepts or rejects. Evangelistic preaching assumes people can genuinely choose Christ if they will.

More Calvinist-leaning fundamentalists emphasize God's sovereign election. Before creation, God chose specific individuals for salvation based solely on His good pleasure, not foreseen faith or merit. The atonement's benefits apply only to the elect. The Spirit irresistibly draws chosen individuals to salvation. This framework limits human free will significantly regarding salvation's ultimate outcome, though maintaining that individuals freely choose according to their nature. The unregenerate freely choose sin because sin enslaves them; the regenerate freely choose righteousness because grace transforms them.

Both perspectives agree that believers possess genuine agency in sanctification and service. Christians must choose daily to deny self, take up the cross, and follow Christ. The imperatives throughout epistles command volitional obedience: put off the old self, put on the new; flee youthful lusts; pursue righteousness; study Scripture; pray without ceasing. These commands presume believers can obey or disobey.

The urgency of gospel proclamation intensifies because souls' eternal destinies hang in the balance. Hell awaits those who reject Christ; heaven welcomes those who believe. Time is short—Christ could return imminently. Fundamentalists preach with urgency, calling sinners to immediate decision. Altar calls invite public profession because choices matter eternally.

Biblical authority remains paramount. Whatever Scripture teaches about divine sovereignty and human responsibility must be accepted, even if philosophical reconciliation eludes us. Romans 9 emphasizes God's sovereign election; John 3:16 offers salvation to "whoever believes." Both are true because Scripture is inerrant.

Practically, fundamentalists emphasize personal evangelism, separation from worldliness, and doctrinal purity. Believers choose whom to befriend, what to consume, and where to worship—choices with spiritual consequences. Free will, understood within inerrancy's framework, enables the faithful, separated, soul-winning life fundamentalism demands.
