*** 1.7.151 Sikhism Response

Sikhism understands space (akash) as a divine manifestation within the all-encompassing reality of Waheguru, neither independently real nor merely illusory, but rather a functional dimension through which God's creation unfolds and through which spiritual ascent occurs. Space serves simultaneously as cosmic arena, contemplative metaphor, and spiritual pathway toward union with the Formless One.

The foundational text, Guru Granth Sahib (compiled 1604), presents space through Guru Nanak's (1469-1539) cosmological hymns, particularly in Japji Sahib where countless regions and universes exist within divine consciousness. Guru Arjan (1563-1606) developed this understanding in Sukhmani Sahib, describing how divine light pervades all spatial dimensions. Guru Gobind Singh (1666-1708) in Jaap Sahib emphasized God's transcendence beyond spatial categories while simultaneously filling all space.

The Mool Mantar establishes that Ik Onkar (One Reality) is "Akal" (timeless) and thus beyond conventional space-time, yet the Guru Granth Sahib repeatedly describes creation spanning "countless regions, countless skies" (asankh desh, asankh akash). This paradox resolves through understanding space as simultaneously real at the phenomenal level and transcended at the ultimate level. Guru Nanak's cosmology describes multiple khands (realms)—Dharam Khand, Gian Khand, Saram Khand, Karam Khand, and Sach Khand—representing progressive spiritual regions rather than merely physical locations.

Key arguments include: (1) space as divine hukam (order/command), reflecting God's will rather than independent existence; (2) spatial metaphors expressing spiritual states, where "inner space" of the heart becomes more significant than external dimensions; (3) space as path for Nam (divine Name) to resonate, making it essentially sonic-spiritual rather than merely geometric.

The Guru Granth Sahib states: "In the realm of truth dwells the Formless Lord, having created the creation, He watches over it" (Japji Sahib, Pauri 37), suggesting space exists within divine awareness. Another passage notes: "The sky and the earth, the nether regions, infinite are His beings" (GGS, p. 4), acknowledging spatial vastness while attributing it to divine creativity.

Internal variations exist between scholarly interpretations emphasizing metaphorical readings versus more literal cosmological understandings. The Namdharis and Nirankaris stress purely spiritual interpretations, while traditional commentators maintain both literal and metaphorical dimensions.

Critics questioning how formless God relates to spatial creation receive the response that divine transcendence includes rather than excludes immanence—God is "nirankar" (formless) yet pervades all "akar" (form).

Contemporary Sikh thinkers like Sirdar Kapur Singh (1909-1986) have engaged quantum physics, suggesting Sikh cosmology anticipated non-local, consciousness-based models of space. Ecological Sikhi movements emphasize sacred space in environmental contexts.

Compared to Advaita Vedanta's maya-based illusionism, Sikhism maintains space's relative reality while subordinating it to divine will, similar to Islamic occasionalism but with stronger pantheistic elements.

*** 1.7.152 Sant Mat Response

Sant Mat tradition conceptualizes space (akash/gagan) as a multilayered reality extending from gross physical dimensions through progressively subtle inner regions (lok/mandal), ultimately culminating in purely spiritual domains beyond all spatial characteristics. Space functions primarily as the structural framework for the soul's return journey from material entanglement to its original home in Sach Khand.

The medieval Sant poets including Kabir (1440-1518) and Guru Nanak (1469-1539) established foundational cosmology, describing inner regions accessed through surat shabd yoga (union through sound current). Tulsi Sahib of Hathras (1763-1843) systematized Sant Mat cosmology, delineating specific regions and their characteristics. Shiv Dayal Singh (Soami Ji Maharaj, 1818-1878) further refined this understanding, particularly distinguishing purely spiritual realms from psycho-physical ones.

Sant Mat identifies distinct spatial regions: the physical universe (Pind), the astral plane (Anda), the causal plane (Brahmanda), and purely spiritual regions (Sach Khand and beyond). Each region possesses its own spatial characteristics, with lower realms bound by conventional dimensionality while higher regions transcend geometric constraints. The Sar Bachan (Soami Ji's teachings) describes how shabd (divine sound current) creates and sustains these regions as vibrational manifestations.

Philosophical arguments include: (1) Space as graduated emanation—denser at material levels, increasingly subtle at higher planes, ultimately dissolving in pure consciousness; (2) Experiential verification through inner spiritual practice allows direct perception of these regions; (3) The sound current (shabd) precedes and structures space, making space derivative rather than fundamental.

Kabir's poems declare: "The true home is beyond the three worlds; where there is neither sun nor moon, there the essence-less essence resides." Tulsi Sahib writes: "Beyond Trikuti lies the realm of pure spirit, where space dissolves into divine radiance."

Internal variations exist regarding the precise number and nomenclature of inner regions. Some lineages identify five primary regions; others enumerate seven or more sub-divisions. The Radha Soami tradition emphasizes systematic mapping, while other Sant lineages stress direct experience over cosmological detail.

Critics questioning whether these "inner spaces" represent actual metaphysical regions or psychological states receive varying responses. Orthodox Sant Mat maintains their objective reality, independent of individual consciousness, verified through consistent reports across practitioners. Progressive interpreters suggest they represent states of consciousness spatially metaphorized.

Contemporary Sant Mat teachers like Maharaj Charan Singh (1916-1990) have addressed scientific cosmology, suggesting inner regions exist in dimensions beyond physical space-time. The tradition emphasizes that intellectual understanding remains secondary to experiential realization through meditation practice.

Compared to Theosophy's astral planes, Sant Mat cosmology maintains stronger soteriological focus, viewing spatial regions primarily as waypoints in spiritual liberation. Unlike Buddhist rejection of substantial regions, Sant Mat affirms their relative reality while maintaining only the highest spiritual realm is eternal.

*** 1.7.153 Radha Soami Response

The Radha Soami tradition understands space as a hierarchically structured emanation from the Supreme Being (Anami Purush), consisting of multiple spiritual and material regions (desh/lok) through which the current of shabd (divine sound) flows, creating and sustaining progressively denser levels of spatio-temporal manifestation. Space is neither independently real nor purely illusory but exists as a functional reality within the grand hierarchy of creation.

Shiv Dayal Singh (Soami Ji Maharaj, 1818-1878), founder at Agra, established the systematic cosmology detailing fourteen regions organized into four major divisions. His successor Rai Saligram (Huzur Maharaj, 1829-1898) further elaborated this framework. Baba Jaimal Singh (1838-1903) established the Beas branch, whose leaders including Maharaj Sawan Singh (1858-1948) provided extensive commentaries on spatial metaphysics, particularly in "Philosophy of the Masters."

The Sar Bachan presents the definitive cosmology: Par Brahm (the spiritual-material divide) separates purely spiritual regions (Sach Khand, Alakh, Agam, Anami) from regions containing spatial characteristics (Brahm's domain, the universal mind region, astral and physical planes). Each region possesses distinct spatial properties—the physical universe follows Euclidean geometry, astral regions exhibit fluid dimensionality, causal planes transcend conventional space, while purely spiritual realms exist beyond all spatial categories.

Core arguments include: (1) Space as shabd manifestation—divine sound current creates vibrational patterns that congeal into spatial forms at progressively denser frequencies; (2) Consciousness primacy—space exists within consciousness rather than consciousness within space; spatial experience changes fundamentally as awareness ascends through meditation; (3) Guru as spatial navigator—the living Master guides disciples through inner regions that, while real, remain inaccessible without proper guidance.

Soami Ji writes: "The Shabd has created all regions and universes; it sustains them and will ultimately withdraw them." The Prem Bani states: "Countless universes exist within each region; the Supreme transcends them all."

Internal variations exist particularly between the Agra, Beas, and Dayalbagh branches. Agra tradition emphasizes philosophical precision in cosmological mappings. Beas focuses on practical meditation over cosmological detail. Dayalbagh incorporates more scientific discourse, attempting harmonization with modern physics.

Critics questioning the epistemological basis for claims about inaccessible inner regions receive the response that Sant Satgurus possess direct experiential knowledge verified through their spiritual realization, similar to how scientists trust expert testimony in specialized fields.

Contemporary applications include Beas leader Maharaj Charan Singh's (1916-1990) dialogues with scientists, suggesting inner regions might correlate with quantum field dimensions. The tradition maintains that space exploration reveals only physical space's vastness, not the infinitely more significant inner dimensions.

Compared to Theosophy's seven planes, Radha Soami maintains more regions with greater specificity. Unlike Advaita's illusionism, it affirms relative reality of spatial regions while maintaining only the supreme region is permanent.

*** 1.7.154 Eckankar Response

Eckankar conceptualizes space as manifestation of divine ECK (the audible life current) across multiple planes of existence, understanding spatial dimensions as vibrational frequencies rather than geometric extensions, with soul's journey through these planes constituting spiritual evolution toward God-Realization. Space functions as both educational arena and vibrational spectrum through which consciousness travels in its return to the Ocean of Love and Mercy.

Paul Twitchell (1908-1971), Eckankar's founder in 1965, synthesized various Sant Mat and Theosophical teachings to create a distinctive cosmology presented in "The Spiritual Notebook" and "The Shariyat-Ki-Sugmad." Darwin Gross (1928-2008), second Living ECK Master, emphasized experiential aspects of inner planes travel. Harold Klemp (1942-present) has provided contemporary refinements, particularly addressing scientific understandings of dimensional space.

Eckankar identifies multiple planes: Physical, Astral, Causal, Mental, Etheric (Soul Plane), and several purely spiritual realms beyond. Each plane represents not merely a location but a vibrational state, with space itself being the ECK's manifestation at particular frequencies. The Shariyat-Ki-Sugmad (Eckankar's scripture) describes how divine sound and light create spatial matrices at each level.

Philosophical foundations include: (1) Space as vibrational spectrum—different "spaces" represent consciousness vibrating at different frequencies, similar to how different radio frequencies carry distinct information; (2) Soul travel primacy—consciousness is not confined to physical spatial coordinates but can navigate inner dimensions through spiritual exercises; (3) Living ECK Master as wayshower—provides guidance through spatial regions that physical senses cannot access.

Twitchell writes in The Shariyat: "The universes are but thought forms in the mind of the SUGMAD, held in space by the ECK." Another passage states: "Soul travels the high road to God through inner space, not outer space."

Internal variations exist regarding literalistic versus symbolic interpretations of inner planes. Earlier teachings emphasized detailed mapping of inner regions with specific characteristics. Contemporary presentations sometimes employ more metaphorical language, speaking of "states of consciousness" rather than separate spatial locations.

Critics questioning the evidential basis for claims about astral and causal planes are addressed through emphasis on personal spiritual experience. Eckankar maintains that soul travel provides direct verification, comparable to how one verifies dream states through personal experience rather than external measurement.

Contemporary applications include framing ancient teachings in modern scientific language—speaking of "dimensions" and "frequencies" rather than traditional cosmological terms. This appeals to scientifically-minded seekers while maintaining core metaphysical claims. Environmental consciousness emerges from understanding physical space as sacred manifestation.

Compared to Theosophy, Eckankar emphasizes individual spiritual experience over hierarchical structure. Unlike Sant Mat's emphasis on the living human guru, Eckankar's Living ECK Master functions more as teacher than absolute authority. Similar to Radha Soami but with more Western esoteric influences integrated.

*** 1.7.155 Transcendental Meditation Response

Transcendental Meditation (TM) tradition, founded by Maharishi Mahesh Yogi, understands space as an expression of the unified field of pure consciousness, simultaneously existing as manifest physical extension and unmanifest pure potentiality. Space represents the "gap" between consciousness and matter, ultimately reconcilable through understanding both as expressions of the same transcendental reality described in Vedantic philosophy and increasingly validated through quantum physics.

Maharishi Mahesh Yogi (1918-2008) synthesized Advaita Vedanta, particularly as taught by his master Swami Brahmananda Saraswati, with modern physics, especially in "Science of Being and Art of Living" (1963) and later works on Vedic Science. Tony Nader (1955-present), current leader, has extensively developed correlations between consciousness and physical structures in "Human Physiology: Expression of Veda and Vedic Literature" (1995), including spatial manifestations.

The tradition employs the concept of "akasha" from Vedic texts, understood as both physical space (bhuta-akasha) and consciousness-space (chid-akasha). The Rig Veda's cosmological hymns and Upanishadic teachings on Brahman as simultaneously transcendent and immanent provide scriptural foundation. Maharishi emphasized that during transcendental consciousness, the meditator experiences the source of space—the unified field where space emerges from non-spatial pure consciousness.

Core arguments include: (1) Space as frozen consciousness—physical space represents consciousness in manifest form, with meditation reversing this process to experience pre-spatial awareness; (2) Unified field correlation—modern physics' search for a unified field corresponds to Vedantic pure consciousness from which space-time emerges; (3) Hierarchical manifestation—space exists at multiple levels: physical/sensory, mental/subtle, transcendental/causal, with TM providing access to increasingly fundamental levels.

Maharishi taught: "Space is the blueprint of matter; pure consciousness is the blueprint of space." The Mandukya Upanishad's teaching that Turiya (fourth state) transcends spatial manifestation while containing all space supports this view.

Internal variations exist between emphasizing phenomenological experience versus theoretical cosmology. Early TM teaching stressed practical meditation benefits over metaphysical speculation. Later Maharishi Vedic Science extensively developed theoretical frameworks attempting to demonstrate correspondence between Vedic cosmology and quantum physics, sometimes creating tension with practitioners preferring simpler presentations.

Critics questioning the scientific validity of consciousness-physics correlations receive responses citing quantum mechanics' measurement problem and observer effects as suggesting consciousness's fundamental role. However, mainstream physicists generally find these correlations speculative.

Contemporary applications include the Global Country of World Peace's promotion of Vastu architecture, understanding space's geometric organization affects consciousness. Research on the "Maharishi Effect" suggests consciousness influences not just inner experience but collective space.

Compared to classical Advaita, TM maintains similar metaphysics but emphasizes accessibility through technique rather than philosophical inquiry. Unlike purely scientific materialism, TM insists consciousness precedes and structures space rather than emerging from spatial arrangements of matter.

*** 1.7.156 ISKCON (Hare Krishna) Response

The International Society for Krishna Consciousness (ISKCON) understands space (akasha) as Krishna's separated energy (shakti), simultaneously real as divine manifestation yet temporary and dependent upon the Supreme Personality of Godhead. Space constitutes one of the eight material elements enumerated in Bhagavad-gita, serving as the subtle container within which gross material creation unfolds across multiple planetary systems within infinite universes.

A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada (1896-1977), ISKCON's founder, established this understanding through translations and commentaries on Bhagavad-gita (1968), Srimad Bhagavatam (1972-1977), and other Vaishnava texts. He drew upon the Gaudiya Vaishnava lineage through Bhaktisiddhanta Sarasvati (1874-1937), who emphasized Chaitanya Mahaprabhu's (1486-1534) theological personalism. Contemporary ISKCON scholars like Satyaraja Dasa (Steven Rosen) have engaged modern cosmology while maintaining traditional frameworks.

Bhagavad-gita 7.4 establishes the foundational taxonomy: "Earth, water, fire, air, ether (space), mind, intelligence and false ego—all together these eight constitute My separated material energies." Space (akasha/vyoman) represents the most subtle of the gross elements, possessing the quality of sound (shabda) as its distinctive characteristic. The Srimad Bhagavatam's cosmological sections (particularly Canto 3 and 5) detail how universal space emerges from the unmanifest pradhana through sequential evolution guided by Krishna's glance.

ISKCON maintains multiple spatial frameworks: (1) Vertical cosmology—fourteen planetary systems (lokas) arranged hierarchically from hellish regions through earthly to heavenly and transcendental realms; (2) Horizontal infinity—unlimited material universes, each enclosed in shells of elements, floating in the Causal Ocean; (3) Transcendent dimension—Vaikuntha planets exist beyond material space entirely, characterized by eternality rather than temporal-spatial constraints.

Philosophical arguments include: (1) Space as Krishna's energy demonstrates both real manifestation and ultimate dependence—it's not maya (illusion) but it's not independent reality; (2) Personalist hierarchy—space serves conscious purposes, facilitating karmic justice and devotional cultivation across different planetary environments; (3) Qualitative distinctions—material space differs fundamentally from spiritual space, which is chinmaya (composed of consciousness) rather than jada (inert).

Bhagavad-gita 9.10 states: "This material nature, working under My direction, O son of Kunti, is producing all moving and unmoving beings." Srimad Bhagavatam 3.26.33 explains: "Akasha, or space, is generated from the subtle element intelligence, and from space comes the quality of sound."

Internal variations exist regarding cosmological literalism. Traditional ISKCON maintains Bhagavatam cosmology as literally true, including geocentric planetary arrangements. Progressive members suggest symbolic or alternative scientific interpretations. Scientist-devotees like Sadaputa Dasa (Richard Thompson, 1947-2008) attempted reconciliation with modern astronomy.

Critics questioning apparent conflicts with observational cosmology receive varied responses. Conservatives maintain that modern instruments observe only gross physical dimensions, missing subtle material and spiritual realms. Moderates suggest different reference frames or dimensional perspectives.

Contemporary applications include Vedic Planetarium project visualizing Bhagavatam cosmology and eco-theological movements emphasizing sacred space in environmental protection.

Compared to impersonal Advaita, ISKCON maintains space's relative reality as divine energy. Unlike Western theism's creatio ex nihilo, space emerges through emanation while remaining Krishna's energy.

*** 1.7.157 Brahma Kumaris Response

Brahma Kumaris World Spiritual University conceptualizes space through its distinctive "Trimurti" cosmology, understanding physical space as the temporary stage for the eternal world drama (kalpa), while emphasizing a transcendent "incorporeal world" (Paramdham) that exists beyond spatial dimensions, accessible through soul consciousness and meditation. Individual members vary between literal and symbolic interpretations of the subtle region.

Critics questioning the astronomical impossibility of a 5,000-year-old universe receive responses distinguishing between the current cycle's age and eternal cyclic repetition. The movement maintains scientific evidence addresses only physical manifestation, not eternal metaphysical structure. Historical contradictions with the claimed timeline are explained as confusion between cyclical ages.

Contemporary applications include Raja Yoga meditation centers worldwide emphasizing the practical benefit of accessing "incorporeal consciousness" rather than defending cosmological details. The movement's focus on world transformation appeals to those seeking personal empowerment beyond traditional religious cosmologies.

Compared to Hindu cosmology's vast temporal scales (yugas spanning millions of years), Brahma Kumaris presents a compressed timeframe. Unlike most traditions treating space as fundamental, they subordinate it to consciousness and cyclical time, similar to some interpretations of Buddhist time-primacy but with eternal repetition replacing progressive liberation.

*** 1.7.158 Ananda Marga Response

The Brahma Cakra (Cosmic Cycle) describes how consciousness descends into matter through progressive densification within spatial matrices, then reverses through spiritual evolution.

They exist at the intersection of mind and matter, suggesting space is ultimately consciousness-derived rather than independently existing.

Philosophical arguments include: (1) Relational emergence—space arises from the relationship between Purusha and Prakriti rather than being self-subsistent; (2) Hierarchical density—space manifests at multiple levels from mental to physical, with meditation allowing access to increasingly subtle spatial experiences; (3) Evolutionary function—space provides the dimensional framework for microcosmic entities to evolve toward macrocosmic unity.

Internal variations exist between emphasizing traditional Tantric cosmology versus Sarkar's innovative microvita theory. Orthodox members maintain strict fidelity to Sarkar's teachings, while progressive interpreters engage contemporary physics more freely, sometimes suggesting quantum foam or string theory might correlate with microvita-structured space.

Critics questioning empirical verification of microvita receive responses that mainstream science hasn't developed instruments subtle enough to detect them, similar to historical periods before microscopes revealed microorganisms. The theory's testability remains controversial even within Ananda Marga.

Contemporary applications include PROUT (Progressive Utilization Theory) economic philosophy, which extends spatial thinking to social organization—advocating for socio-economic regions based on psycho-economic principles rather than arbitrary political boundaries.

Compared to Samkhya's Prakriti-based cosmology, Ananda Marga maintains similar dualism but ultimately resolves into qualified non-dualism. Unlike purely materialist space-time, Ananda Marga insists consciousness precedes spatial manifestation while avoiding Advaitic complete illusionism.

Compared to pure Advaita's world-negation, Ramakrishna Mission maintains stronger affirmation of spatial reality for practical purposes. Unlike dualistic traditions, ultimate identity between Brahman and space is maintained, accessible through both philosophical inquiry and devotional realization.

Contemporary applications include SRF's emphasis on scientific spirituality—encouraging members to view space not as dead matter but as living divine vibration. This perspective influences environmental awareness, with nature's spaces understood as sacred manifestations deserving reverence.

Compared to traditional Advaita Vedanta, SRF maintains similar metaphysics but emphasizes accessibility through technique (Kriya Yoga) over philosophical understanding. Unlike purely scientific materialism, consciousness precedes and structures space. Similar to Christian mysticism's "God in all things" but with more detailed cosmological-energetic framework.

*** 1.7.162 Satya Sai Baba Movement Response

The Satya Sai Baba movement understands space (akasha) as the first and subtlest of the five great elements (panchabhutas), emerging from the absolute Brahman through the creative principle of Maya, serving simultaneously as cosmic principle in external creation and meditation object in internal spiritual practice. Space functions as the divine garment through which formless Divinity assumes forms, with Sai Baba himself claimed as the Avatar who both transcends spatial limitation and manifests within it for devotees' liberation.

Satya Sai Baba (1926-2011) of Puttaparthi taught through discourses, declaring himself the avatar of the age combining Shiva-Shakti principles. His teachings synthesized Advaita Vedanta's non-dualism with devotional theism, presented in accessible language in collections like "Sathya Sai Speaks" (30 volumes, 1953-2011). Scholarly interpreters like Dr. Samuel Sandweiss and Dr. John Hislop systematized his cosmological teachings, while Professor N. Kasturi served as official biographer documenting his views on space and creation.

Sai Baba's cosmology follows traditional Vedantic sequence: from formless Brahman emerges Maya (the creative power), from which space arises as the first differentiation, possessing sound (shabda) as its quality. From space emerges air, fire, water, and earth in succession. However, Sai Baba emphasized that all elements including space are ultimately consciousness assuming forms through the power of sankalpa (divine will).

Philosophical frameworks include: (1) Hierarchical manifestation—space represents the initial descent from absolute to relative, the first concretization of infinite consciousness; (2) Sound correlation—space and sound are intrinsically connected, with OM (pranava) being both the source and substance of space; meditation on subtle sound reveals space's true nature; (3) Avatar presence—the Avatar simultaneously exists beyond all spatial-temporal limitations while manifesting within space for the world's welfare, demonstrating space's subordination to consciousness.

Sai Baba taught: "The cosmos is my body; space is my form. Where is space absent? It is omnipresent, so too am I." Another discourse states: "Space is not emptiness but the divine presence in its subtlest form."

Internal variations exist between emphasizing philosophical teachings versus miraculous demonstrations. Some followers focus on Sai Baba's discourses explaining Vedantic principles of space, while others emphasize reported materializations and spatial manipulations (appearing in multiple locations, producing objects from empty space) as validating his claims about space's consciousness-based nature. Academic devotees prefer philosophical systematization; mass devotion centers on miraculous narratives.

Critics questioning materialization phenomena and other spatial manipulations receive varied responses. Believers maintain these demonstrate mastery over space's vibratory nature, similar to yogic siddhis described in Patanjali's Yoga Sutras. Skeptics within the movement suggest focusing on spiritual teachings rather than miraculous claims.

Contemporary applications include the extensive educational and medical institutions created by the movement, understood as manifesting divine consciousness in physical space through service. Architecture of Prasanthi Nilayam and other centers attempts to create sacred spatial environments conducive to spiritual awareness.

Compared to traditional Advaita, Sai Baba movement maintains similar metaphysics but with stronger avatar-centric devotional framework. Unlike impersonal approaches, divine personality is emphasized as pervading and transcending space simultaneously.

*** 1.7.163 Jainism Response

Jainism understands space (akasha) as one of five fundamental uncreated eternal substances (dravyas), existing independently and absolutely as the medium enabling location and movement, divisible into lokakasha (inhabited universe) and alokakasha (uninhabited void beyond). Space is neither created by nor dependent upon consciousness, souls, or divinity, but functions as the permanent substrate accommodating all entities—souls, matter, time, and motion principles—in their eternal transformations.

The ancient Jain tradition, systematized by Mahavira (599-527 BCE), received cosmological elaboration in canonical texts including Tattvarthasutra by Umasvati (c. 2nd-5th century CE) and Tiloyapannatti. Medieval scholars including Kundakunda (2nd-3rd century CE), Haribhadra (8th century), and Hemachandra (1089-1172) further refined spatial metaphysics. Contemporary Jain scholars like Nathmal Tatia and John Koller have engaged Western philosophy to articulate Jain spatial realism.

Jain ontology identifies six substances (dravyas): jiva (soul), ajiva (non-soul), which subdivides into pudgala (matter), dharma (medium of motion), adharma (medium of rest), akasha (space), and kala (time). Space is unique in being infinitely extensive while providing the necessary condition for all other substances' existence. Lokakasha, the finite inhabited universe, has a specific geometry described in Jain cosmography—resembling a cosmic person (lok-purusha) with three main divisions: upper worlds (urdhva-loka), middle worlds (madhya-loka), and lower worlds (adho-loka). Beyond lokakasha extends infinite alokakasha, empty of all substances except space itself.

Philosophical arguments include: (1) Substance-quality realism—space is not relational or emergent but an independently existing substance possessing the quality of providing accommodation (avagahana); (2) Infinity and finitude—space itself is infinite, but the occupied universe (lokakasha) is finite, with the boundary established by the extent of dharma-dravya (medium of motion); (3) Indivisibility debate—while space can be conceptually divided into regions (pradesha), it lacks the atomic structure of matter, existing as continuous substrate.

Tattvarthasutra states: "Akasha is that which provides room" (5.17). Another sutra elaborates: "Space is one substance but has infinite spatial points (pradesha)."

Internal variations exist between Digambara and Svetambara schools, though both maintain similar spatial metaphysics. The primary difference concerns cosmographical details and the precise geometry of lokakasha. Contemporary Jain thinkers debate whether to interpret traditional cosmography literally or symbolically given astronomical discoveries. Traditionalists maintain its literal truth in subtle dimensions, while progressives suggest metaphorical interpretation.

Critics questioning how uncreated eternal substances relate to Big Bang cosmology receive varied responses. Some Jains distinguish between subtle eternal reality and gross periodic manifestations. Others suggest modern cosmology observes only material transformations within eternal spatial substrate. The tradition's commitment to spatial absolutism creates tension with relativistic physics.

Contemporary applications include environmental philosophy emphasizing space's sacred character—all spatial regions potentially house life forms deserving protection. Jain cosmopolitan ethics extends from recognizing infinite souls inhabiting various spatial regions.

Compared to Vedantic illusionism, Jainism maintains robust spatial realism. Unlike Abrahamic creation from nothing, space is eternal and uncreated. Similar to Newtonian absolute space but with metaphysical pluralism acknowledging multiple eternal substances.

*** 1.7.164 Digambara Jainism Response

Digambara Jainism maintains the orthodox Jain position on space (akasha) as an eternal, uncreated, independently existing substance (dravya), while emphasizing particularly rigorous philosophical analysis of spatial infinitude, the precise demarcation between lokakasha and alokakasha, and the relationship between spatial accommodation and the principles of motion and rest. The Digambara school stresses absolute metaphysical realism regarding spatial substance against any idealist or phenomenalist reductions.

The Digambara ("sky-clad") tradition traces to the ancient schism (c. 4th century BCE) with distinctive philosophical development through Kundakunda (c. 2nd century CE), whose Samayasara and Pravachanasara address spatial metaphysics. Akalanka (8th century) and Vidyanandi (9th century) developed sophisticated logical defenses of spatial realism against Buddhist momentariness and Vedantic illusionism. Modern scholars like Champat Rai Jain (1867-1942) presented Digambara cosmology to English audiences.

Digambara texts elaborate the six-substance ontology with particular precision regarding space. Space differs from the five other substances (souls, matter, dharma, adharma, time) in being the only infinitely pervasive substance. While lokakasha (inhabited universe) is finite—containing exactly 343 cubic rajju (cosmic units) arranged in the cosmic person (lok-purusha) configuration—space itself extends infinitely as alokakasha beyond. This creates the paradox of finite occupation within infinite substrate, resolved by understanding dharma-dravya (motion principle) as extending only to lokakasha's boundary.

Philosophical arguments include: (1) Substance-mode distinction—space as substance (dravya) is eternal and unchanging, but its modes (paryaya) manifest as various regions accommodating different entities; (2) Accommodation as essential quality—space's defining characteristic is avagahana (providing room), which is neither empty void nor positive medium but sui generis ontological function; (3) Pluralistic realism—against monistic collapse of substances into one reality, Digambara philosophy maintains seven independent eternal categories including space.

Kundakunda's Samayasara states: "Space is that which provides accommodation to souls, matter, and principles of motion and rest." The Tattvarthasutra (accepted by Digambaras) asserts: "The substances dharma, adharma, and jivas (souls) have the same extent as the universe-space (lokakasha)" (5.7).

Internal Digambara variations exist between Mula Sangha and other sub-sects primarily on monastic practice rather than spatial metaphysics. However, contemporary Digambara scholars debate whether traditional cosmography should be maintained literally or reinterpreted symbolically. Traditionalists like Kailash Chand Jain insist on literal truth, while progressive scholars suggest allegorical readings compatible with modern astronomy.

Critics questioning how eternal infinite space relates to expanding universe cosmology receive the response that modern science observes material transformations within permanent spatial substrate. The expansion of matter-energy doesn't require spatial creation, only redistribution within eternal space. Some suggest lokakasha itself might expand and contract while alokakasha remains infinite.

Contemporary applications include environmental ethics rooted in spatial sacredness. Recognizing that all space potentially accommodates infinite life forms generates reverence for environmental protection. Digambara emphasis on non-possessiveness extends to spatial resources.

Compared to Svetambara Jainism, Digambara maintains similar spatial metaphysics but with stricter philosophical rigor and less willingness to accommodate scientific revisions. Unlike Hindu idealism, robust realism is non-negotiable. Similar to ancient atomists' spatial absolutism but with pluralistic substance ontology.

*** 1.7.165 Svetambara Jainism Response

Svetambara Jainism upholds the fundamental Jain understanding of space (akasha) as an eternal, uncreated substance providing accommodation for all other entities, while demonstrating somewhat greater flexibility in cosmographical interpretation and engaging more extensively with contemporary scientific cosmology. The tradition maintains space's ontological independence while allowing symbolic readings of traditional spatial descriptions.

The Svetambara ("white-clad") lineage developed distinct canonical texts following the schism, with cosmological works including the Jambudvipa-prajnapti and Sthananga Sutra. Medieval scholars like Haribhadra (8th century), Hemachandra (1089-1172), and Yashovijaya (1624-1688) refined spatial metaphysics while engaging competing philosophical schools. Contemporary scholars like Nathmal Tatia (1908-1990) and Padmanabh Jaini (1923-2021) have extensively interpreted Svetambara cosmology for modern audiences.

Svetambara cosmology maintains the six-substance framework with space as eternal substrate, accepting the traditional description of lokakasha organized into upper, middle, and lower worlds. However, Svetambara commentators have shown more willingness to distinguish between empirical-pedagogical descriptions and ultimate metaphysical principles. The inhabited universe's precise geometric configuration receives multiple interpretations across Svetambara sub-sects (Murtipujaka, Sthanakvasi, Terapanthi), with increasing emphasis on symbolic rather than strictly literal readings.

Philosophical arguments include: (1) Accommodative function—space's essential nature is providing avagahana (room) for substances without being modified by them, similar to how a container accommodates contents; (2) Anekantavada application—the doctrine of non-absolutism (anekantavada) allows multiple valid perspectives on space: empirical, metaphysical, soteriological; (3) Jnana-based understanding—right knowledge of spatial reality, including its eternality and substance-nature, contributes to liberation by eliminating false views.

The Tattvarthasutra states: "Akasha is that which gives space" (5.18). Hemachandra's Yogashastra notes: "Understanding space's true nature—neither created nor destroyed—frees the soul from attachment to spatial locations."

Internal Svetambara variations are significant. Murtipujaka (image-worshipping) communities maintain more traditional cosmographical views, often preserving temple diagrams of the cosmic person. Sthanakvasi and Terapanthi reform movements, emphasizing ethical practice over cosmology, tend toward symbolic interpretations. Contemporary Svetambara scholars engage quantum physics and relativity, suggesting possible correspondences while maintaining core metaphysical commitments to spatial eternality and independence.

Critics questioning astronomical contradictions with ancient cosmography receive more accommodating responses in Svetambara tradition than Digambara. Scholars distinguish between the eternal metaphysical principle (space as independent substance) and specific cosmographical descriptions, which served pedagogical functions for ancient audiences. The essential teaching—space's eternality, independence, and accommodative function—remains while cosmographical details allow reinterpretation.

Contemporary applications include Jain ecology movements emphasizing space's sacred character and non-possessiveness regarding spatial resources. The Sthanakvasi and Terapanthi sub-sects particularly emphasize ethical implications over cosmological speculation. Svetambara diaspora communities in the West often present spatial teachings through dialogue with physics, emphasizing Jain cosmology's sophisticated engagement with infinity and substance theory.

Compared to Digambara Jainism, Svetambara maintains identical core metaphysics but demonstrates greater interpretive flexibility. Unlike Buddhist emptiness (shunyata), Jain space is robustly real. Similar to Vaiseshika atomism's spatial container but with eternality extending to multiple substances, not just space.
