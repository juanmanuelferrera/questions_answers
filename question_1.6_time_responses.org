* Question 1.6: What is the nature of time?

*** 1.6.1 Catholic Response

The Catholic philosophical tradition maintains that time constitutes the measure of motion in material substances (mensura motus secundum prius et posterius), existing as a real but dependent aspect of created reality that participates analogically in divine eternity. Following Aristotelian-Thomistic principles, time is neither a self-subsistent entity nor a mere subjective illusion, but rather an accident of material being, specifically the numbered measure of change according to before and after. God, as ipsum esse subsistens (subsistent being itself), transcends temporal succession entirely, existing in eternity understood as the simultaneous and perfect possession of unlimited life (tota simul et perfecta possessio interminabilis vitae), while creation participates in existence through temporal succession, moving from potentiality to actuality in measured progression toward eschatological fulfillment.

The historical development of Catholic temporal philosophy evolved through distinct periods. Augustine of Hippo (354-430) established foundational principles in his Confessions (Book XI), arguing that time exists as a distention of the soul (distentio animi), with past existing in memory, present in attention, and future in expectation, while God's eternity encompasses all temporal moments simultaneously without succession. Boethius (480-524) refined the concept of eternity in The Consolation of Philosophy (Book V), defining divine eternity as "the complete possession all at once of illimitable life" and establishing the classical distinction between eternal divine existence and temporal creaturely being. Thomas Aquinas (1225-1274) achieved systematic synthesis in Summa Theologiae (I, q.10) and Commentary on Aristotle's Physics (IV), integrating Aristotelian natural philosophy with Christian theology, understanding time as the numbering of motion according to before and after, requiring both a counting intellect and numbered change, with angelic duration (aeviternity) mediating between divine eternity and creaturely temporality.

Catholic temporal metaphysics advances several interlocking arguments. First, the motion-measurement theory establishes that time exists as the intellectual measure of physical change: since motion involves successive actualization of potencies, and succession implies ordering according to before and after, time emerges as the numbered measure of this succession. Second, the participation framework explains temporal existence as imperfect participation in divine eternity: creatures exist through successive moments because their finite being cannot grasp unlimited existence simultaneously, while God's infinite being possesses all perfections eternally without temporal extension. Third, the soul-body analysis demonstrates that human temporal experience combines sensory apprehension of physical change with intellectual measurement: the corporeal senses register successive states while the intellect numbers and measures progression, enabling authentic temporal knowledge. Fourth, eschatological teleology orients temporal existence toward eternal fulfillment: creation moves through time toward its divinely intended completion when temporal succession transforms into eternal participation in beatific vision.

Key textual foundations include Scripture's revelation that God exists beyond temporal limitation ("I am the Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the end" - Revelation 21:6; "With the Lord one day is as a thousand years, and a thousand years as one day" - 2 Peter 3:8), while creation operates in temporal succession ("In the beginning God created" - Genesis 1:1). The Fourth Lateran Council (1215) dogmatically affirmed God's eternal nature transcending temporal creation. Aquinas's technical apparatus distinguishes eternity (aeternitas) as lacking all succession, aeviternity (aeviternitas) as having fixed being with accidental succession, and time (tempus) as involving substantial succession in being.

Internal variations exist within Catholic thought. Neo-Platonic Augustinians emphasize subjective temporal distension and divine illumination over Aristotelian objective measurement. Scotistic voluntarism stresses divine will's role in temporal ordering rather than necessary participation structures. Suarezian scholasticism treats time as extrinsic denomination rather than real measure. Contemporary phenomenologists like Edith Stein engage Husserlian time-consciousness while maintaining realist foundations. Process-influenced thinkers like John Haught explore divine temporality while preserving classical commitments.

Catholic temporal philosophy addresses multiple counter-arguments. Against absolute temporal realism claiming time exists independently of change, Thomists demonstrate that time requires motion as its substrate, existing as measure rather than self-subsistent reality. Against pure conventionalism reducing time to human construction, Catholic realists argue that temporal succession exists objectively in material change, with human intellect discovering rather than creating temporal order. Against process theology's temporal God, classical theists maintain that divine eternity transcends temporal becoming without negating intimate providence, with God's eternal decree encompassing all temporal moments without undergoing change. Against Kantian subjectivism treating time as mere mental form, neo-Scholastics demonstrate that temporal ordering exists in things themselves, with subjective apprehension corresponding to objective reality.

Contemporary applications address modern scientific and philosophical challenges. Relativity theory's demonstration that temporal measurement varies with gravitational fields and velocity confirms time's dependence on material conditions while complicating but not contradicting the substance-accident framework. Quantum indeterminacy raises questions about temporal becoming and causation addressed through potency-act analysis refined for probabilistic actualization. Debates about temporal passage (A-theory versus B-theory) find Catholic resources supporting dynamic becoming through act-potency metaphysics while acknowledging eternal divine perspective encompassing all temporal moments. Environmental theology employs temporal teleology to ground cosmic purpose and ecological responsibility within creation's temporal unfolding toward divinely intended fulfillment.

Compared to Protestant sola scriptura approaches emphasizing biblical temporal revelation, Catholic natural theology maintains philosophical demonstration of temporal principles through reason. Unlike Eastern Orthodox apophatic emphasis on incomprehensible divine transcendence, Thomistic analogical predication affirms positive knowledge of God's eternity. Unlike process theology's dipolar God with temporal pole, classical theism maintains absolute divine immutability. This sophisticated temporal metaphysics continues providing frameworks for scientific engagement, theological development, and philosophical analysis of reality's temporal dimensions.

*** 1.6.2 Protestant (Reformed) Response

Jonathan Edwards (1703-1758) contributed sophisticated philosophical analysis in Freedom of the Will and The End for Which God Created the World, arguing that God's eternal purpose determines temporal existence entirely while maintaining authentic creaturely agency within secondary causation. Herman Bavinck (1854-1921) synthesized classical Reformed positions in Reformed Dogmatics, emphasizing organic development of redemptive history through temporal covenantal progression.

Reformed temporal theology advances several core arguments. First, the sovereignty-temporality thesis establishes that divine eternal decree determines all temporal events: God's comprehensive foreordination means nothing occurs by chance or autonomous temporal causation, with every moment serving predetermined divine purposes. Second, covenant-historical progression structures temporal understanding: history moves through covenantal administrations (creation, Noah, Abraham, Moses, David, Christ) revealing progressive unfolding of eternal redemptive purpose, with temporal sequence displaying God's pedagogical wisdom. Third, eternal-temporal asymmetry maintains God's transcendence: while God comprehends all time eternally without succession, creatures experience genuine temporal progression, with divine eternity encompassing rather than negating created temporality. Fourth, eschatological teleology orients temporal existence toward God's glory: history advances inevitably toward consummation when temporal kingdoms yield to eternal divine kingdom, demonstrating God's sovereign purpose in creation.

The Canons of Dort (1618-1619) defend unconditional election occurring in eternity before temporal creation, with temporal calling and glorification following predetermined divine purpose.

Internal Reformed variations exist regarding specific temporal questions. Supralapsarians place election logically before the fall in the eternal decree's ordering, while infralapsarians reverse this sequence, though both maintain comprehensive divine sovereignty. Some Reformed theologians adopt occasionalism, denying genuine secondary temporal causation, while mainstream Calvinism affirms concurrence: divine and creaturely causes cooperate without competition. Open theists within broader evangelical Calvinism challenge exhaustive divine foreknowledge, arguing for dynamic temporal divine engagement, though classical Reformed theology rejects this as compromising sovereignty. Postmillennialists envision gradual temporal progress toward Christianized society before Christ's return, while amillennialists spiritualize millennial reign, and premillennialists anticipate dramatic eschatological intervention disrupting historical progression.

Reformed theology addresses counter-arguments systematically. Against Arminian claims that divine foreordination negates human freedom, Reformed theology distinguishes compatibilist freedom: humans act according to their desires without external compulsion, with divine sovereignty operating through rather than against creaturely willing. Against process theology's temporal God learning from creation, Reformed theology maintains absolute divine immutability: God's eternal knowledge encompasses all temporal events without acquiring new information or undergoing change. Against open theism's claim that genuine relationship requires temporal divine responsiveness, classical Calvinism argues that eternal divine decree provides more secure foundation for providence than temporal divine reactivity. Against deistic separation of eternal God from temporal world, Reformed theology emphasizes intimate providence: God's eternal decree executes through continuous sustaining activity maintaining temporal existence.

Contemporary applications address modern challenges. In science-theology dialogue, Reformed thinkers engage quantum indeterminacy and chaos theory while maintaining comprehensive divine sovereignty operating through natural laws. In historical theology, covenant theology provides framework for understanding progressive revelation's temporal unfolding. In practical piety, divine sovereignty over temporal events provides comfort amid suffering: nothing occurs outside God's purpose, with temporal trials serving eternal sanctification. In social ethics, Abraham Kuyper's sphere sovereignty applies Reformed principles to temporal institutions, with diverse social spheres possessing God-ordained temporal authority within respective domains.

Compared to Catholic philosophical temporal analysis, Reformed theology emphasizes scriptural over philosophical foundations. Unlike Arminian synergism affirming libertarian freedom within temporal causation, Calvinism maintains monergistic salvation executing eternal election. Unlike Eastern Orthodox mystical temporal participation, Reformed theology emphasizes forensic justification's eternal basis manifesting temporally. Unlike dispensationalist sharp temporal distinctions between Israel and Church, covenant theology sees organic temporal continuity. This robust temporal theology continues shaping Reformed worship, ethics, and cultural engagement grounded in divine sovereignty over all temporal existence.

*** 1.6.3 Lutheran Response

Luther's Heidelberg Disputation (1518) established theology of the cross: God reveals Himself sub contrario (under opposites) in temporal suffering and weakness rather than eternal glory directly perceived. His Large Catechism (1529) grounded sacramental understanding in historical-temporal events: baptism connects believers to Christ's historical death and resurrection, while Eucharist makes Christ's sacrificial body and blood truly present despite temporal separation from Calvary. Johann Gerhard (1582-1637) systematized Lutheran orthodoxy in Loci Theologici, defending ubiquity doctrine: Christ's glorified humanity participates in divine omnipresence, enabling sacramental real presence throughout temporal and spatial creation.

Lutheran temporal theology advances several distinctive arguments. First, the incarnational-temporal principle establishes that eternity enters time definitively in Christ: God doesn't merely act upon temporal creation from eternal transcendence but assumes temporal existence personally, with Incarnation revealing time's theological dignity as bearer of eternal presence. Lutheran theologians debate predestination's temporal implications: some emphasize eternal election's security, others stress temporal faith's necessity, with Formula of Concord maintaining dialectical tension avoiding both determinism and synergism.
