*** 1.6.151 Sikhism Response

Sikhism conceives time as a divine manifestation flowing from Akal Purakh (the Timeless One), simultaneously transcendent and immanent within creation. Time functions as both māyā (illusion) requiring transcendence and divine lila (play) through which Nam (the Divine Name) pervades existence. This paradoxical understanding—time as both obstacle and opportunity—characterizes Sikh temporal theology.

Guru Nanak (1469-1539) established foundational temporal concepts in the Japji Sahib, describing God as beyond temporal categories yet manifesting through historical revelation. The compilation of the Guru Granth Sahib (1604) by Guru Arjun crystallized diverse temporal perspectives, including contributions from Kabir and Namdev. Guru Gobind Singh (1666-1708) emphasized cyclical-linear synthesis, viewing history as repeating patterns culminating in Khalsa realization.

The Mul Mantar opens with "Ik Onkar" (One Reality), described as "Akal Murat" (beyond time's form), establishing temporal transcendence as foundational. The Japji Sahib declares: "Countless worlds, countless times and ages" (asankh jug asankh bhaar), positioning time as infinite divine expression. The Adi Granth emphasizes living in haumai-free present awareness while recognizing karmic temporal continuity.

First, Sikhism distinguishes between Kaal (measured time) and Akaal (timelessness). Human existence unfolds in Kaal, subject to birth-death cycles, while Nam simran (remembrance) provides access to Akaal consciousness. Second, the sant-sipahi (saint-soldier) ideal requires engaged temporal action informed by timeless truth. Third, the doctrine of miri-piri (temporal-spiritual authority) integrated by Guru Hargobind affirms time's spiritual significance, rejecting pure escapism.

The Guru Granth Sahib declares: "In the True Lord's sanctuary, time holds no power" (SGGS 12), while simultaneously affirming: "As mortals plant, so shall they harvest" (karmi āpo āpani, SGGS 4), balancing transcendence with temporal accountability. The Rahiras prayer describes God as "kaal rahit" (beyond time) yet governing temporal cycles.

Internal variations exist between Khalsa orthodox emphasis on historical-prophetic time versus Udasi ascetic focus on mystical timelessness. The Namdhari tradition emphasizes continuing Guru succession, extending sacred time, while mainstream Sikhism closed revelation with the Guru Granth Sahib.

Critics question how genuine timelessness reconciles with historical revelation and community memory. Sikhism responds that divine timelessness manifests through temporal forms without contradiction, similar to wave-particle complementarity in physics.

Contemporary Sikhs apply temporal theology to environmental stewardship (recognizing deep time responsibility) and social justice (understanding historical patterns requiring intervention). Diaspora communities negotiate traditional cyclical festival time with linear secular calendars.

Compared to Vedantic illusionism, Sikhism grants greater reality to temporal existence. Unlike Abrahamic linear eschatology, Sikh thought synthesizes cyclical rebirth with progressive historical revelation.

Contemporary applications include values-based education (teaching eternal principles within temporal curriculum), medical and humanitarian service (expressing timeless compassion in temporal suffering), and interfaith dialogue (recognizing eternal truth underlying diverse temporal religious forms).

Compared to traditional Hinduism's cyclic avatars, Sai Baba's self-proclaimed status generated controversy. Unlike Christianity's single incarnation, Hinduism accepts multiple avatars across time, though specific claims require faith.

*** 1.6.163 Jainism Response

Jainism conceives time as beginningless and endless eternal substance (dravya) distinct from but interacting with consciousness (jiva), matter (pudgala), space (akasha), and other fundamental realities. Time flows in massive cosmic cycles (kalachakra) without creator deity, operating according to natural law rather than divine will. Unlike moksha (liberation) which transcends karmic bondage, time itself continues eternally—providing framework within which souls achieve liberation and new souls enter bondage.

Ancient Tirthankaras including Parshvanatha (traditional dating 877-777 BCE) and Mahavira (599-527 BCE) established Jain cosmology, refined through centuries by philosopher-monks including Umasvati (c. 150 CE), author of Tattvarthasutra, and Kundakunda (c. 200 CE). Medieval scholars Haribhadra (8th century) and Hemacandra (12th century) developed sophisticated temporal metaphysics distinguishing Jain atomistic realism from Buddhist momentariness and Hindu illusionism.

The Tattvarthasutra defines time (kala) as substance enabling modification and persistence, distinct from consciousness yet real. Uttaradhyayana Sutra describes cosmic cycles: descending arc (avasarpini) of progressive degeneration and ascending arc (utsarpini) of improvement, each containing six ages spanning billions of years. Liberation (kevala jnana) transcends temporal causation without annihilating time itself.

First, Jain metaphysics affirms time's substantial reality (dravya) rather than mere relational property or illusion. Time exists as real substance enabling change, providing sequential framework independent of consciousness. Second, the doctrine of anekantavada (many-sidedness) applies to time: from one perspective (naya), time flows irreversibly; from another, eternal substances persist unchanged; both perspectives are partially valid. Third, karma manifests as actual material particles binding consciousness across temporal spans; liberation requires temporal process of karmic exhaustion through asceticism and right conduct.

Uttaradhyayana Sutra declares: "The wheel of time revolves eternally—neither created nor destroyed." Tattvarthasutra states: "Time is that which enables continuity and succession" (5.22).

Internal variations exist between Digambara emphasis on absolute renunciation transcending temporal concerns and Svetambara allowing greater engagement with temporal community life. Scholarly Jains debate whether time is single substance or multiple atomic time-points. Mystical traditions emphasize timeless kevala-jnana, while ritual-oriented communities focus on auspicious temporal observances.

Critics question how truly substantial time differs from relational time in explanatory power. Jains respond that substantiality explains time's irreversible flow and resistance to consciousness manipulation—unlike idealist frameworks suggesting consciousness creates time.

Contemporary applications include environmental ethics based on eternal karmic consequences, animal rights activism (ahimsa applied across deep time), and dialogue with physics regarding time's fundamental versus emergent nature.

Compared to Buddhist momentariness (kshanika-vada), Jain realism affirms perduring substances within time. Unlike Advaita's maya, Jain realism grants full ontological status to temporal existence.

*** 1.6.164 Digambara Jainism Response

Digambara Jainism conceives time with particular emphasis on absolute renunciation's transcendence of temporal concerns, while maintaining fundamental Jain cosmological framework of eternal time cycles (kalachakra) and substantial time (kala dravya). The Digambara ("sky-clad") tradition emphasizes that complete liberation requires absolute detachment from temporal attachments, including clothing, manifesting in their distinctive nude ascetic practice. Time functions as testing ground where souls demonstrate readiness for moksha through progressively radical renunciation.

The Digambara lineage traces to early Jain community divisions (c. 300 BCE), crystallizing distinct identity through the philosopher-monks Kundakunda (c. 2nd century CE), whose Samayasara and Pravachanasara emphasize transcendent perspective beyond temporal causation, and Umasvati's Tattvarthasutra (accepted by both Digambara and Svetambara). Medieval scholars Akalanka (8th century) and Vidyanandi (9th century) developed rigorous logical defenses of Jain temporal realism against Buddhist and Hindu critiques.

Digambara texts emphasize the Samayasara's teaching: "From the transcendent perspective (nishchaya naya), the soul is eternally pure consciousness; from practical perspective (vyavahara naya), souls undergo temporal bondage and liberation." The Satkhandagama (Six-part Scripture) describes precise measurements of cosmic time cycles spanning countless billions of years, with current era in fifth stage of descending cycle.

First, Digambara philosophy rigorously applies anekantavada (perspectivism) to temporality: from absolute viewpoint, pure soul (shuddha jiva) transcends temporal modification, eternally existing as consciousness-knowledge; from practical viewpoint, empirical soul (vyavahara jiva) undergoes karmic bondage across temporal spans. Neither perspective is complete alone. Second, the nude ascetic practice manifests temporal transcendence—rejecting future-oriented planning (accumulating possessions) and past-attached identity (clothing signifying social roles). Third, the doctrine emphasizes that moksha, while transcending temporal causation, requires temporal process; instant liberation without karmic exhaustion is impossible.

The Samayasara declares: "The soul in its pure form is beyond time, space, and causation, yet the empirical soul moves through time." Tattvartha Sutra states: "Right belief, knowledge, and conduct constitute the path to liberation"—temporal journey toward timeless realization.

Internal variations exist between scholarly Digambaras emphasizing philosophical understanding and ascetic practitioners focusing on practical renunciation. The Kanji Swami movement (20th century) emphasized Kundakunda's transcendent perspective, generating controversy about proper balance between worldly engagement and detachment. Terapanth and Taranpanth subgroups developed distinct temporal practices.

Critics question whether nude asceticism genuinely transcends temporal concerns or merely substitutes different temporal focus. Digambaras respond that the practice manifests internal detachment visibly, serving pedagogical purpose for both practitioner and observers.

Contemporary Digambara communities balance traditional cosmology (including vast temporal cycles contradicting modern chronology) with scientific engagement, some scholars proposing symbolic interpretation of time scales. Lay Digambaras negotiate ascetic temporal ideals with householder responsibilities.

Compared to Svetambara Jainism, Digambara emphasizes more radical renunciation. Unlike Hindu sannyasa allowing orange robes, Digambara practice manifests absolute poverty including temporal identity markers.

*** 1.6.165 Svetambara Jainism Response

Svetambara Jainism conceives time through the same fundamental Jain cosmology of eternal cycles (kalachakra) and substantial time (kala dravya) while emphasizing community engagement and gradual spiritual progress within temporal existence. The Svetambara ("white-clad") tradition allows monks and nuns to wear white garments, symbolizing acknowledgment of temporal existence's practical demands while pursuing liberation. Time functions as extended opportunity for spiritual development through community support, ethical discipline, and progressive purification.

The Svetambara lineage traces to early Jain community divisions (c. 300 BCE), with distinct canonical texts including the Agamas preserved through oral tradition and written down (5th century CE). Key philosophers include Haribhadra Suri (8th century), whose Yogadrishtisamuccaya synthesized diverse spiritual paths, and Hemacandra (1088-1172), whose Yogashastra integrated temporal ethics with liberation pursuit. The tradition subdivided into Murtipujaka (image-worshipping), Sthanakvasi (non-image), and Terapanth sects.

Svetambara Agamas describe cosmology with precise temporal measurements: current avasarpini (descending) half-cycle will last 21,000 years total, now in fifth age of increasing degradation before ascending cycle begins. The Kalpa Sutra, describing Mahavira's life, emphasizes temporal biographical details situating liberation within historical context rather than transcendent timelessness alone.

First, Svetambara philosophy balances transcendent liberation with temporal engagement, allowing monks/nuns to accept alms, travel during non-monsoon seasons, and maintain community institutions—all requiring temporal planning. Liberation remains goal, but temporal means involve sustainable community practices. Second, the tradition emphasizes lay participation in spiritual life through support of ascetics and observance of limited vows (anuvrata), creating temporal merit (punya) gradually preparing for full renunciation in future lives. Third, elaborate temporal ritual calendar (including Paryushana festival) structures spiritual practice through sacred time, unlike pure timeless meditation emphasis.

Uttaradhyayana Sutra states: "Time flows eternally like a wheel; some souls ascend toward liberation, others descend into bondage." Haribhadra writes: "The path unfolds gradually through time; sudden liberation comes only to rare souls with minimal karmic residue."

Internal variations exist between Murtipujaka temples maintaining elaborate temporal ritual schedules versus Sthanakvasi rejection of images focusing on inner development, and between Terapanth's centralized organization under single Acharya versus decentralized Murtipujaka sanghas. The Kanji Swami controversy involved Svetambara-born reformer advocating Digambara perspectives.

Critics question whether temporal community engagement compromises renunciation ideal. Svetambaras respond that sustainable spiritual practice requires community infrastructure, and gradual path acknowledges practical reality of most practitioners.

Contemporary Svetambara communities actively engage environmental protection (applying ahimsa across temporal scales), animal welfare, and interfaith dialogue. The tradition produces significant scholarship reconciling traditional temporal cosmology with modern scientific chronology, often through symbolic interpretation.

Compared to Digambara radical detachment, Svetambara pragmatism allows greater temporal engagement while maintaining liberation as ultimate goal. Unlike Hindu grhastha-sannyasa sharp division, Svetambara envisions continuity between lay and ascetic temporal lives.
