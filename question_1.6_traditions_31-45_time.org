*** 1.6.31 Mu'tazila Response

Time constitutes a created accident ('arad) distinct from eternal divine essence, possessing objective reality as the measure of motion and change, with human rational faculties capable of apprehending temporal relationships independently of revelation. This rationalist position emerged from early Islamic kalam (speculative theology) during the Abbasid period (8th-9th centuries CE), particularly through the systematization of Wasil ibn 'Ata (d. 748 CE) and Abu al-Hudhayl al-'Allaf (d. 841 CE), who developed sophisticated arguments distinguishing divine eternity (qidam) from created temporality (huduth). The Mu'tazilites grounded their temporal philosophy in Qur'anic affirmations of divine unity and justice (Qur'an 112:1-4, 21:23), arguing that time's created nature preserves God's absolute transcendence while enabling meaningful human moral agency.

Mu'tazilite temporal theory rests on three core arguments: First, the atomistic argument positing time as composed of discrete indivisible moments (آنات, anat) corresponding to atomic theory of matter, enabling precise analysis of causation and divine action. Second, the createdness argument (huduth al-zaman) demonstrating time's contingency through the impossibility of infinite temporal regress, proving divine creative priority. Third, the justice argument connecting temporal succession with human free will and moral responsibility, requiring genuine temporal passage for meaningful ethical action. The Baghdad school, particularly al-Nazzam (d. 835 CE), developed sophisticated theories of temporal continuity challenging atomistic models, while the Basra school emphasized discrete temporal atoms preserving divine omnipotence.

Internal variations include debates between atomists and continualists regarding temporal structure, with figures like Abu Hashim al-Jubba'i (d. 933 CE) developing intermediate positions. Mu'tazilites addressed counter-arguments from Ash'arites who claimed their temporal theory limited divine sovereignty, responding that genuine temporal succession actually manifests divine wisdom in ordering creation rationally. Contemporary applications include Islamic rationalist approaches to modern physics and cosmology, with Mu'tazilite temporal frameworks providing resources for dialogue with scientific understanding of spacetime while maintaining theological commitments. The tradition shares rationalist methodology with Jewish Karaite and Christian Scholastic temporal theories, particularly in employing logical demonstration to reconcile created temporality with divine eternity, though Mu'tazilites uniquely emphasize human rational autonomy in temporal comprehension.
