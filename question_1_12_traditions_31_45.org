* Question 1.12: Is truth absolute or relative?

** Tradition 31: Methodist (Holiness)

Truth is absolute in its divine source while demanding radical personal appropriateness in sanctification. The Holiness movement affirms that God's nature establishes unchanging moral and spiritual realities—the holiness of God, the sinfulness of humanity, and the necessity of entire sanctification constitute absolute truths transcending cultural variation. Wesley's doctrine of Christian perfection presupposes objective standards against which believers measure spiritual progress. However, we recognize that human apprehension of truth develops progressively through sanctifying grace. The heart made perfect in love perceives spiritual realities more clearly than the unregenerate mind, suggesting experiential dimensions to truth-encounter.

The complexity emerges in applying absolute truth to varied circumstances. While lying remains absolutely wrong, the Spirit may guide sanctified believers to express truth differently across situations—not relativizing truth itself but personalizing its communication. Phoebe Palmer's altar theology emphasized immediate, complete consecration to absolute truth, yet acknowledged individual journeys toward this standard. The Holiness tradition rejects postmodern relativism that denies objective truth while embracing Wesleyan emphasis on truth transforming individual hearts uniquely.

Scripture provides absolute revelation of God's character and redemptive plan, yet requires Spirit-illumined interpretation. Two sanctified believers may understand particular passages differently without compromising truth's absoluteness—their perspectives represent partial glimpses of comprehensive divine reality rather than contradictory truths. The doctrine of entire sanctification itself demonstrates this balance: the standard of perfect love is absolute and unchanging, yet its manifestation varies according to individual capacity, calling, and circumstance.

Holiness methodology combines revivalist certainty with compassionate humility. Camp meetings proclaimed absolute truth with urgency, calling sinners to immediate decision, yet recognized each person's unique spiritual condition. We affirm moral absolutes—temperance, purity, simplicity—while acknowledging that living these truths requires discernment in complex situations. A tobacco farmer convicted of holiness principles faces different practical considerations than a merchant, though the truth about bodily stewardship remains constant.

Contemporary Holiness adherents navigate tension between maintaining doctrinal absolutes and engaging pluralistic society. We witness to absolute truth about sin, salvation, and sanctification without claiming absolute comprehension of all mysteries. Truth's absoluteness demands our complete devotion; our finite understanding requires ongoing teachability, dependence on Scripture, and openness to deeper Spirit-revelation while never compromising core convictions about God's holy character and transforming power.
