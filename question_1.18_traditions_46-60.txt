*** 1.18.53 SUNNI ISLAM (ASH'ARI) Response

Space represents created accident (arad) dependent entirely upon Allah's continuous sustaining power, possessing no inherent existence independent of divine will. This occasionalist metaphysics emphasizes absolute divine sovereignty over every aspect of spatial existence.

Al-Ash'ari and his successors challenged Mu'tazilite rationalism, which granted secondary causes real efficacy. Ash'aris maintain that apparent spatial relationships—distance, extension, location—manifest divine custom (ada) rather than necessary laws. Bodies don't occupy space through inherent properties but through Allah's creating both atoms and their spatial positioning simultaneously. Space itself comprises accidents adhering to atoms through divine action rather than existing as independent substrate.

Al-Ghazali's occasionalism radically applies this principle. When objects appear separated spatially, this configuration results from Allah's creating discrete atoms in particular arrangements at each instant. Nothing persists between moments through natural continuity; rather, Allah recreates spatial patterns continuously. This understanding preserves divine omnipotence—Allah could instantly rearrange all spatial relationships without violating logical possibility, as miracles demonstrate.

The Qur'an affirms Allah's transcendence beyond spatial limitation: "He is with you wherever you are" (57:4), yet "No vision can grasp Him" (6:103). Ash'ari theology resolves this through establishing that Allah exists without spatial location (bila kayfa), neither occupying space nor existing nowhere. Divine presence means comprehensive knowledge and power throughout creation rather than spatial containment. Allah relates to space as Creator to creation, utterly distinct from creaturely spatial existence.

Al-Baqillani developed atomistic cosmology addressing spatial divisibility. Space comprises indivisible atoms (jawahir fardah) arranged through divine action. This atomic theory prevents infinite divisibility paradoxes while maintaining that spatial structure depends entirely on divine will. Between atoms exists neither void nor substance, only divine creative power holding configurations together.

Ash'ari theology addresses throne (arsh) and footstool (kursi) verses literally while denying spatial containment of Allah. The throne exists as created reality affirming divine majesty without implying Allah occupies spatial position. Divine "sitting" (istawa) upon the throne means sovereign rule rather than physical placement, understood "without asking how" (bila kayfa).

Modern physics doesn't threaten Ash'ari principles. Relativity's space-time unification and quantum indeterminacy actually resonate with occasionalism—showing that spatial properties aren't absolute but relational and probabilistic, consistent with moment-by-moment divine creation. Scientific descriptions capture divine custom's regularity rather than discovering independent natural laws.

Ethically, recognizing spatial reality's dependence on Allah cultivates humility and gratitude. Humans occupy space as trustees (khulafa), responsible for maintaining creation's order according to shari'a guidance. Territorial disputes require justice recognizing ultimate divine ownership.

Eschatologically, Allah will dissolve current spatial order at qiyama, creating renewed heavens and earth for eternal recompense. Space thus serves temporarily as testing ground before eternal spatial configurations of paradise and hellfire.

Salafi scholars condemn philosophical cosmologies—Aristotelian eternal universe, Neoplatonic emanations, materialist determinisms—as corrupting influences contradicting revelation. Space isn't eternal, self-existent, or evolving through impersonal forces but created, contingent, and governed by Allah's decree. Modern Big Bang theories receive scrutiny: if compatible with creatio ex nihilo, potentially acceptable; if implying pre-existing matter or denying divine agency, categorically rejected.

Spatial ethics emphasize monotheism's practical manifestations. Pilgrimage to Mecca demonstrates spatial centrality of tawhid—Ka'ba represents focal point toward which global Muslim community orients daily prayers, physically expressing unified submission. Salafis oppose grave veneration, shrine visitation, and spatial practices suggesting mediators between Allah and believers, viewing such customs as shirk (polytheism) corrupting Islam's original purity.

Contemporary territorial issues demand shari'a application. Palestinian lands' occupation, Rohingya displacement, and Uyghur persecution involve spatial injustices requiring Muslim solidarity. However, violent extremism claiming Salafi justification represents deviance (ghuluw); authentic Salafism emphasizes knowledge-based activism, political engagement within legal frameworks, and avoiding fitnah (discord).

Environmental stewardship follows from recognizing Allah's ownership. Humans serve as khalifah (trustees) managing spatial resources responsibly. Wastefulness (israf), pollution, and ecological destruction violate Islamic principles demanding resource conservation and creation's respectful treatment.

Eschatological spatial transformation will occur exactly as revealed: heavens rolled like scrolls, earth flattened, resurrected bodies gathered in new spatial configuration for judgment, then distributed eternally to paradise or hellfire—all understood literally per salaf methodology.

Ultimately, sound spatial understanding requires purging innovations, adhering strictly to revelation, and following salaf precedent exclusively.

*** 1.18.60 IBADI ISLAM Response
