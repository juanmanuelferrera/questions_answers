*** 1.25.136 Positivism (Comte) Response

Comte's positivism approaches possible worlds through phenomenal laws and instrumental utility: "possible worlds" metaphysically meaningless—positive philosophy rejects unobservable metaphysics, focusing solely on phenomenal regularities (*lois*). This actual observed world provides all legitimate knowledge; speculative alternatives violate positive method restricting claims to observable. Counterfactuals instrumentally useful—"if different initial conditions, then different outcomes" expresses lawful relations enabling prediction (*prévoir pour pouvoir*). Modal language pragmatically serves science but doesn't reference transcendent possibilities. Mathematical possibilities are conceptual tools exploring hypothetical scenarios, not discoveries of Platonic alternatives. Necessity reflects observed invariant regularities, not metaphysical constraints. Only one observable world; multiple theoretical descriptions pragmatically acceptable if equally predictive.

*** 1.25.137 Utilitarianism Response

Utilitarianism approaches possible worlds through consequentialist evaluation and felicific calculus: alternative scenarios assessed by expected utility—which maximizes aggregate happiness? This actual world suboptimal; possible improvements require imagining alternatives, calculating consequences. Counterfactuals essential for moral reasoning—"if different policy, what welfare effects?" Possible worlds serve utilitarian decision-making under uncertainty—probability-weighted outcomes guide choices toward greatest happiness. Modal metaphysics subordinated to practical ethics: possibilities matter insofar as actionable, affecting welfare calculations. Mathematical possibilities enable modeling alternative policies, economic systems, social arrangements. Best possible world = maximal aggregate utility configuration; actual world improvable through rational reform guided by consequentialist analysis of alternatives.

*** 1.25.138 Marxism Response

Dialectical development: contradictions (*Widersprüche*) within capitalism generate possibilities for socialist transformation. Modal space historically-materially constrained: not any social formation possible anytime—productive forces' development level limits viable alternatives. Counterfactuals serve ideological critique and revolutionary strategy: imagining post-capitalist possibilities mobilizes change. Mathematical possibilities reflect economic base—bourgeois mathematics serves capitalist calculation; socialist mathematics would serve collective planning. Possible worlds = alternative class configurations, each with characteristic ideology, social relations.

*** 1.25.139 Pragmatism (Peirce) Response

Possible worlds = alternative actualizations of objective dispositional properties, explored through diagrammatic reasoning (iconic representations). Counterfactuals meaningful: referring to what would occur if antecedent conditions obtained—grounded in real habits/laws, not mere linguistic conventions. Modal realism moderate: generals genuinely exist (*contra* nominalism) as immanent regularities (*contra* Platonism), structuring possibility-space. Mathematical possibilities investigate what necessarily follows from hypothetical conditions—logic's diagrammatic experimentation on icons reveals necessary relations among possibles.

*** 1.25.140 Pragmatism (James) Response

Counterfactuals pragmatically useful but epistemically uncertain: "if X occurred..." guides action though ultimate verification impossible (no access to unactualized alternatives). Mathematical possibilities are useful fictions—conceptual tools organizing experience, not discovered Platonic realities. Modal language's "cash-value" lies in practical consequences for action and experience, not metaphysical correspondence. *** 1.25.142 Phenomenology (Husserl) Response

*** 1.25.143 Existentialism (Kierkegaard) Response

*** 1.25.147 Analytic Philosophy (Frege) Response

Frege approaches possible worlds through logical objectivity and *Gedanken* (thoughts): logical-mathematical truths exist as objective thoughts (*Gedanken*)—timeless, mind-independent, non-psychological—inhabitating third realm (neither physical nor mental). Possible worlds aren't concrete alternative universes (Lewis) but logical spaces—combinations of thoughts' truth-values. This actual world corresponds to true thoughts; possible worlds = consistent assignments of truth-values to elementary thoughts. Counterfactuals express logical relations among thoughts, not causal relations among events. Mathematical possibilities concern formal logical structures—what follows from axioms necessarily. Sense-reference distinction: modal contexts intensional—"possibly P" refers to thought's modal property, not truth-value. Logical necessity absolute, not relative to worlds: contradictions impossible in any consistent assignment. Mathematics discovers objective logical truths, not invented conventions.

*** 1.25.148 Analytic Philosophy (Russell) Response

Russell approaches possible worlds through logical atomism and definite descriptions: ultimate furniture of universe consists of atomic facts (particulars instantiating universals); possible worlds = alternative combinations of atomic facts. This actual world one arrangement; logically possible alternatives require only non-contradictory fact-combinations. Counterfactuals analyzed through descriptions: "if the present King of France were bald" lacks truth-value (empty description). Mathematical possibilities concern logical structures—set theory, numbers as classes of classes. Early Platonist (universals exist independently); later nominalist tendencies. Logical necessity = tautology (truth in all interpretations). Possible worlds useful logical fiction but ontologically, atomic facts suffice—concrete particulars plus universals (if realist phase) or just particulars (if nominalist phase).

*** 1.25.149 Analytic Philosophy (Wittgenstein—Early) Response

Early Wittgenstein (*Tractatus*) approaches possible worlds through logical space and facts: reality = totality of facts (*Tatsachen*), not things. Logical space (*logischer Raum*) contains all possibilities—all combinatorial possibilities of objects. This actual world one point in logical space; possible worlds = alternative fact-configurations. What is the case (*der Fall*) = subset of what could be the case (possibilities). Counterfactuals are senseful (*sinnvoll*) propositions picturing possible but non-actual arrangements. Mathematical propositions are tautologies—true in all possible worlds, saying nothing about world (pseudo-propositions). Logical necessity reflects combinatorial possibilities: contradictions impossible (exclude themselves from logical space); tautologies necessary (include all logical space). Metaphysical modal questions (why this world?) literally nonsensical—bumping against language's limits. Must pass over in silence what cannot be said.

*** 1.25.150 Analytic Philosophy (Wittgenstein—Late) Response

Late Wittgenstein (*Philosophical Investigations*) approaches possible worlds through language-games and forms of life: "possibility" meanings emerge from diverse language-games—scientific, mathematical, ordinary counterfactual talk—no single essence. Mathematical possibility concerns rule-following in formal games—"possible proof" means potential rule-applications, not Platonic discoveries. Necessity = grammatical compulsion within language-game, not metaphysical constraint. Family resemblance: modal concepts overlap without shared essence. Philosophy dissolves modal metaphysics by describing language use, not constructing theories about abstract modal realms.
